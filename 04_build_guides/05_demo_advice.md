# Demo Advice — A City That Tells Its Stories

## The core demo frame for this pillar

Richmond's arts scene is vibrant but invisible to most residents who are not already plugged in. Long-time residents hold irreplaceable neighborhood history that is being lost as neighborhoods change. Both problems are real, both are urgent, and both are solvable with tools a team can build in a weekend.

Start your demo with one of these two sentences (adapt to your specific tool):

**For arts discovery:** "If you moved to Richmond six months ago and wanted to find a gallery opening this weekend, there is no single place to look. We built that place."

**For story collection:** "Richmond's longtime residents carry neighborhood histories that no database contains. We built a way to start collecting those histories before they are gone."

---

## Demo structure (3-5 minutes)

### 1. The moment of failure (30 seconds)
Show the pain point viscerally. For arts discovery: screenshot of a Google search that returns irrelevant results. For story collection: describe the Journey 2 user (75-year-old Church Hill resident) who has no channel to share what she knows.

### 2. The tool (2 minutes)
Live demo with real Richmond data. Do not demo with placeholder text ("Event Title Here") or generic data. Use real:
- Real Richmond event names and venues
- Real Richmond neighborhoods
- Real story prompts that connect to Richmond's specific history

Walk through the core user journey once, cleanly. Do not explore features. Do not explain what you did not build.

### 3. The data layer (30 seconds)
"This data comes from [Eventbrite API + Style Weekly RSS + 1708 Gallery calendar]." Name your sources. Judges respect specificity. "We pull from public sources automatically — no City staff required to maintain it."

### 4. The civic claim (30 seconds)
Keep it narrow and honest: "We believe this tool could reduce the number of Richmond residents who miss arts events they would have attended if they'd known about them." Or: "We believe this is one piece of a larger effort to capture neighborhood histories before they are lost."

Do NOT say: "This will revolutionize how Richmond connects with its arts community." Do NOT claim to solve the whole problem.

### 5. The ask (30 seconds)
"We would want to talk with [The Valentine / Office of Strategic Communications / Chaya Braxton] about whether this tool has a home after the hackathon."

## What to have ready for demo day

### For arts aggregator:
- Pre-fetched dataset of 20+ real Richmond events (in case APIs are slow)
- Working filter for at least date and event type
- 3-5 real source organization names visible in the data
- Mobile view functional

### For story collection portal:
- Working voice recording on at least one device
- 3-5 guided prompts tested and readable
- Consent screen reviewed and plain-language
- One submitted test story visible in an admin view (to show end-to-end)

## Rubric alignment for judges

This pillar scores highest on D8 (Accessibility). Lead with that in your pitch:
- "We designed this for residents who are not digitally comfortable. Here is how: [specific accessibility feature]."
- Voice input, large text, simple navigation, no login required — name what you built.

Other strong rubric dimensions to address:
- D1 (Clarity): Name the exact user and the exact problem in your first sentence.
- D5 (Population & impact): Richmond's arts events reach hundreds of thousands of residents. Neighborhood histories represent decades of lived experience. These numbers are real — use them.
- D7 (Success criteria): Be concrete. "Success means a resident new to Richmond can find a gallery opening in 30 seconds." Not "success means Richmond becomes more connected to its arts."

---

## What to avoid saying in your demo

- "Our AI helps Richmond understand its community." (Too vague and overclaiming)
- "This solves the problem of civic disengagement." (Too large)
- "We scraped data from Facebook Events." (Almost certainly not true and raises policy concerns)
- "This is fully automated." (Probably not — be honest about what is automated vs. seeded)
- "The City can use this tomorrow." (Requires approvals, security review, hosting arrangements — none of which happened in a weekend)
