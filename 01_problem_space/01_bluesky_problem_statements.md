# Blue Sky Problem Statements — A City That Tells Its Stories

These statements were developed before the targeted problem statements. They represent broader, more ambitious framings of the pillar's challenge. They are useful for inspiration and pitch language, but are not the primary focus for weekend MVP development.

Note: "Exploring Richmond Through Its Stories" is the **highest-scoring blue sky statement across all seven pillars** (24/27, Exceptional). It sets the ambition ceiling for this pillar even as the targeted statements define the buildable floor.

---

## Blue Sky 1 — Stories as Civic Insight

Score: 22/27 — Strong

### Statement
How might we use technology to transform the way Richmond collects and learns from residents' lived experiences, so that community stories become a meaningful input to City decision-making, help preserve Richmond's social history, and amplify voices that have traditionally been excluded from formal civic processes?

### Why it scored Strong
- Clear civic value proposition
- Connects collection to use (governance, not just archiving)
- Respects the challenge of underrepresentation
- Implies a sophisticated data and insight layer

### Why it did not score Exceptional
- Data readiness is low (no structured story corpus exists yet)
- The connection between story collection and City decision-making is aspirational rather than operational
- Staff capacity to act on insights is unclear

### Hackathon relevance
The phrase "collection without use is archiving; collection with use is governance" from the working session captures this statement's core insight. A weekend MVP can address either the collection end (portal, guided prompts) or the use end (theme surfacing dashboard) — but probably not both.

## Blue Sky 2 — Exploring Richmond Through Its Stories ★ HIGHEST SCORE ACROSS ALL PILLARS

Score: 24/27 — Exceptional

### Statement
How might we use technology to help residents and visitors explore Richmond's neighborhoods, history, and culture through the stories of the people who shaped them, so that the city's identity becomes richer, more accessible, and more inclusive for everyone who lives here or comes here?

### Why it scored Exceptional
- Broadest possible audience (residents and visitors)
- Strong sense of place (neighborhood-level specificity)
- Combines discovery, history, and cultural tourism
- Accessible framing that does not require formal civic engagement
- Richmond's specific history and neighborhood character are unusually strong assets here

### Why the highest score matters
This is not a coincidence. Richmond has:
- One of the most distinct neighborhood histories in the mid-Atlantic
- A traumatic and transformation-rich civil rights history
- Active oral history institutions (the Valentine, Black History Museum, VCU)
- A arts and music scene that is genuinely neighborhood-rooted
- Rapid neighborhood change that makes the archive-before-loss framing urgent

The Exceptional score reflects that the raw material exists and the civic need is real. The challenge for a hackathon team is to build something that works at the level of a single neighborhood or a single story type — not to "explore all of Richmond."

### Hackathon relevance
This blue sky maps well to:
- A neighborhood history explorer (one neighborhood, real stories, place-based)
- A "story layer" on a map (oral history pins, QR code connection)
- A guided walking-tour style app seeded with real narratives from museum partners
Keep scope to one neighborhood or one theme (e.g., Jackson Ward jazz history, Church Hill community change).

## Blue Sky 3 — Expanding Richmond's Narrative

Score: 17/27 — Needs work

### Statement
How might we use technology to expand the narrative of Richmond beyond its most prominent historical framings, so that the full range of communities, cultures, and voices that make up the city are recognized, preserved, and celebrated?

### Why it scored lower
- Framing is too broad and aspirational without a clear user or mechanism
- "Expanding the narrative" does not point to a specific workflow or tool
- Risk of overpromising what a weekend prototype can accomplish
- No clear data source or starting point

### Hackathon relevance
This blue sky captures an important equity instinct — the concern that dominant narratives crowd out others. That instinct should inform how teams design story collection tools (who gets heard? who is the default user? what languages are supported?). But as a standalone project brief, it needs significant narrowing before it is buildable. Teams drawn to this framing should redirect toward Blue Sky 2 or the Targeted Statement 2 (Resident Stories as Civic Insight) with an explicit equity lens.
