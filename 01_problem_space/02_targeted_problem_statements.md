# Targeted Problem Statements — A City That Tells Its Stories

## 1) Discovering and Participating in Richmond's Arts & Cultural Events

Score: 25/32 — Strong

### Problem statement
How might we use technology to improve how residents discover and engage with Richmond's local arts and cultural events so that locally produced creative work is easier to find, support, and experience, while minimizing manual curation and acknowledging City technology, staffing, and budget constraints?

### Context
Richmond has a vibrant arts and cultural community producing exhibitions, performances, festivals, and creative gatherings. Event information is scattered — galleries, arts organizations, nonprofits, and individual artists maintain separate calendars and promote through social media or informal networks. No broad view of what is happening exists. Manually compiling a comprehensive event calendar would require ongoing staff time the City cannot sustain. The opportunity is to surface and connect what already exists: aggregating rather than replacing.

### Constraints
- Focus on aggregating or syncing existing event information, not recreating content
- Work within City technology policies and approval processes
- Avoid solutions that depend on heavy manual curation or ongoing staff management
- Be cost-effective and sustainable over time
- Respect preferences of partner organizations whose event information may be surfaced

### Success would mean
- Residents can easily discover arts and cultural events happening across Richmond
- Local artists and venues gain greater visibility without additional administrative work
- Event information stays reasonably up to date through automated or lightweight updates
- City staff are not required to manage a large content operation
- Participation in Richmond's arts districts and cultural events increases

- Whether Richmond.com, Style Weekly, or RVA Magazine maintain event listings with accessible feeds
- Arts & Cultural Districts boundary data (if it exists in Richmond GeoHub)
- No arts/cultural event data in Richmond's open data catalog
- How residents currently discover events: undocumented
- Output type unspecified

### Example project directions
- Richmond Arts Aggregator (pull from Eventbrite API, RSS feeds, gallery websites)
- "What's On in RVA" Discovery Feed (automated, filtered by neighborhood/type/date)
- Arts & Culture Event Map (geographic, links to original sources)

## 2) Resident Stories as Civic Insight

Score: 25/32 — Strong

### Problem statement
How might we use technology to improve how Richmond gathers and learns from residents' lived experiences so that community stories help inform City decisions, preserve Richmond's social history, and amplify voices that are often underrepresented, while respecting privacy, trust, and limited staff capacity?

### Context
Richmond's identity is shaped by the lived experiences of its residents — particularly long-time community members, neighborhood leaders, and activists. Many of these narratives are not captured, surfaced, or connected to how the City plans for the future. Existing engagement tools (surveys, public comment periods, 311) collect feedback about specific issues, not broader lived experiences. Museums and cultural institutions preserve oral histories, but those efforts are not connected to City decision-making. Richmond is experiencing rapid neighborhood change, and the stories of long-time residents — particularly from underrepresented communities — risk being lost.

### Constraints
- Respect privacy, consent, and appropriate handling of sensitive stories
- Build trust with residents about how their stories will be used
- Avoid heavy manual transcription, editing, or data processing
- Work within limited City staffing capacity and budgets
- Complement — not duplicate — oral history efforts led by museums and cultural organizations

### Success would mean
- Residents have a clear, trusted way to share their lived experiences with the City
- Stories from underrepresented communities are captured before they are lost
- City staff and leaders can identify themes and insights across many narratives
- Community stories help inform policy, programming, and commemorative efforts
- Richmond's civic storytelling becomes inclusive, visible, and connected to decision-making

### Gaps to close
- Missing: list of existing oral history collections at Richmond museums (the Valentine, Black History Museum, Virginia Museum of History and Culture): scope, format, accessibility
- Any existing resident feedback data the City has collected
- Community organizations doing story-based work (StoryCorps affiliates, local documentary groups, neighborhood history projects)
- Comfortable storytelling channels for target population: undocumented
- Literacy and tech access barriers for target population: undocumented
- Output type unspecified

### Example project directions
- Voice-to-Story Portal (record or type your story, guided prompts, consent-first)
- "Tell Us Your Story" Guided Interface (structured prompts, mobile-friendly)
- Story-to-Insight Dashboard (theme extraction and pattern surfacing from submitted stories)
