# Pillar Overview — A City That Tells Its Stories

## Purpose
This repository is a markdown knowledge base for teams working on the A City That Tells Its Stories pillar in the Richmond Civic Hackathon. It helps teams understand the problem space, review problem statements, explore public data, and shape realistic weekend MVPs.

## Pillar framing
A City That Tells Its Stories focuses on helping Richmonders discover, share, and connect with the cultural and narrative life of their city, including:
- arts and cultural event discovery and aggregation
- civic storytelling and resident voices in City decision-making
- neighborhood history preservation, especially as Richmond changes rapidly
- oral history access and connection to civic processes
- amplifying underrepresented voices in community narratives
- reducing the invisibility of Richmond's creative life to residents who would engage if they could find it

## Why this matters
Richmond's arts scene is vibrant, varied, and largely invisible to residents who do not already know where to look. Event information is scattered across dozens of websites, social feeds, and informal networks. At the same time, the lived experiences of long-time residents — especially from communities facing rapid neighborhood change — are at risk of being lost because no trusted, low-barrier mechanism exists to capture them and connect them to how the City plans its future.

These are not niche problems. They affect:
- residents who want to participate in Richmond's creative and civic life
- artists and venues who need broader reach without adding administrative burden
- elderly and long-time residents whose neighborhood knowledge is time-sensitive
- underrepresented communities whose stories are not captured by existing formal processes
- City planners and communicators who make decisions with incomplete community input

## Useful prototype directions
Prototypes that often help in this pillar:
- aggregate and surface existing event information without recreating it
- make arts and cultural discovery frictionless for residents new to Richmond
- provide a trusted, low-barrier channel for residents to share neighborhood stories
- help staff and decision-makers identify themes across many narratives
- connect oral history collections to community use cases

## Less useful directions
Prototypes that tend to struggle in a weekend:
- require ongoing manual curation by City staff
- claim to represent "all" Richmond voices or be statistically representative
- replace or duplicate museum and archive oral history programs without partnership
- require official City endorsement of story content
- make policy determinations from narrative data
- depend on private or proprietary data
