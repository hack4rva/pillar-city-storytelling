# Pillar 7, Powered: The Stakeholder Map to Launch and Sustain Richmond Storytelling Prototypes

## Executive Summary

To successfully launch and sustain hackathon prototypes under Pillar 7 ("A City That Tells its Stories"), teams must navigate a specific ecosystem of institutional gatekeepers, community champions, and city infrastructure in Richmond, Virginia. Institutional assets are ready-to-use if you align to existing pipelines: VCU Libraries actively hosts public digital collections, including over 600 vintage Richmond postcards and 7,000+ architectural slides, and runs a Community Digitization Program with standard agreements [1] [2] [3]. The Valentine is signaling openness to community-centered storytelling partnerships, having recently acquired the *Style Weekly* photograph archive and initiated a Collection Refinement Initiative under Deputy Director of Collections Meg Hughes [4] [5] [6]. 

City communications amplification is centralized through the Office of Strategic Communications (OSC), which manages public information, FOIA requests, and brand resources [7] [8]. Ethical and rights guardrails are non-negotiable for public memory work, requiring clear consent and content flagging. On-the-ground activations depend on Parks & Recreation timelines for special events [9]. By leveraging named institutional champions like Meg Hughes and Chaya Braxton [10] [11], and utilizing cross-institution content plumbing, teams can reduce technical debt and ensure compliance gaps do not derail otherwise strong ideas.

## Purpose and Scope

This report delivers a comprehensive stakeholder map designed to de-risk and accelerate Pillar 7 prototypes. We focus on identifying who can unlock assets, provide legitimacy, and offer distribution channels—as well as who has the power to block progress—so that prototypes can transition from weekend demos to durable community platforms.

## Pillar 7 Context and Target Problems

Under the strategic umbrella of "A City That Tells its Stories," Pillar 7 prototypes will likely center on resident storytelling access, historical archiving, and community activation. While the specific problem statements must be confirmed with hackathon sponsors at kickoff, the overarching goal is to democratize Richmond's narrative infrastructure. 

## Stakeholder Universe

The stakeholder universe for Pillar 7 encompasses seven key categories that shape access, legitimacy, and reach. Primary users include arts-curious residents and longtime resident storytellers. Secondary beneficiaries range from local artists to venues and arts organizations. Institutional partners like The Valentine, VCU Libraries, and the Office of Strategic Communications (OSC) hold the keys to historical assets and public distribution. Named champions such as Meg Hughes and Chaya Braxton provide critical entry points, while city departments like Parks & Recreation and Planning govern physical and regulatory spaces.

## Institutional Anchors With Ready Assets

VCU Libraries and The Valentine possess accessible pipelines and content. Aligning with their established rules allows hackathon teams to move quickly and securely.

### VCU Libraries: Community Digitization and Archives

VCU Libraries offers robust hosting and agreements that can significantly reduce risk and cost for prototypes. Their digital collections include the "Rarely Seen Richmond" archive of over 600 vintage postcards (1900-1930) and the Richmond Commission of Architectural Review Slide Collection featuring over 7,000 color images [2] [3]. Crucially, they operate a Community Digitization Program that provides a sample digitization agreement for community partners [1]. Teams should adopt their metadata standards early and contact the Publishing team to utilize these existing repositories.

### The Valentine: Collections Access and Curation

The Valentine is actively curating Richmond's zeitgeist and is open to collaboration. In 2021, the museum acquired the *Style Weekly* photograph archive, leading to a major 2024 exhibition co-curated by Meg Hughes, Deputy Director of Collections [5]. The museum is also undergoing a Collection Refinement Initiative to focus strictly on the Richmond region [6]. Prototypes should aim to co-design pilots around underused Richmond assets, defining rights and credit clearly with Hughes.

## City Gateways & Governance

Navigating city infrastructure is essential for official amplification and avoiding bureaucratic red tape.

### Office of Strategic Communications (OSC)

Founded in 2021 and currently led by Director Ross Catrow, the OSC is the central hub for public information, PIOs, FOIA compliance, and brand resources [7] [8]. Aligning messaging and preparing FOIA-ready data maps will speed up city approvals. Teams should book a brand/PIO briefing to ensure compliance.

### Parks & Recreation and Planning

The city's Special Event Planning is managed through Parks & Recreation [9]. Delays here risk public demos, so teams must file permits early or line up controlled venues (like museum courtyards) as backups. Additionally, the Planning department's historical tie-ins (evidenced by the architectural review slides [3]) require consultation for place-based activations.

## Media Ecosystem

*Style Weekly*, recently revived by VPM, offers a credible arts and culture channel for prototype launches [5]. Pitching a feature in sync with a beta launch, coordinated through OSC press channels, can serve as a massive distribution multiplier.

## Risks, Constraints, and Derailers

| Risk Factor | Evidence | Impact | Mitigation Strategy | Owner |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Content Sensitivity** | VCU notes collections may contain offensive/negative stereotypes [2] | Community backlash, loss of trust | Implement content warnings and community review boards | Product Lead |
| **Rights & Clearances** | Valentine's strict collection philosophy [6] | Legal action, takedown notices | Pre-clear image rights; use standard VCU agreements | Legal/Ops |
| **FOIA Obligations** | OSC mandates FOIA compliance for public records [7] | Administrative burden, forced data exposure | Build FOIA-ready documentation from day one | Tech Lead |

*Compliance gaps in rights, branding, or permitting are the most likely factors to quietly derail otherwise strong prototypes.*
