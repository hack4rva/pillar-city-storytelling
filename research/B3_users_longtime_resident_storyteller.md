# Unlocking Church Hill’s Living Memory — A trust-first, offline-ready path to engage longtime residents

## Executive Summary
Capturing the rich, lived history of Richmond's East End requires overcoming severe, intersecting barriers of age, race, and digital access. For our primary persona—a 75-year-old African American woman from Church Hill—standard digital data collection tools will systematically fail. In key East End census tracts, up to 66.2% of households lack broadband internet and 47.7% lack smartphones [1]. Furthermore, Black Richmond residents are 115% more likely to lack internet access and 138% more likely to be unbanked compared to the citywide average [1]. 

To successfully engage this irreplaceable demographic, strategy must pivot from "digital-first" to "trust-first and offline-ready." This means deploying voice-based hotlines, in-person pop-up recording booths at trusted community anchors like The Market @ 25th, and paper-based mail-in kits. Incentives must be immediate and cash-equivalent (like grocery gift cards) to bypass banking barriers. By co-locating capture efforts within familiar neighborhood routines and partnering with established institutions like VCU Libraries and The Valentine for preservation, we can ethically archive six decades of neighborhood memory before it is lost to time and gentrification.

## Purpose and Stakes — Capturing irreplaceable neighborhood memory before it’s lost
Church Hill elders hold high-context, place-based knowledge accumulated over six decades. They have witnessed the dramatic transformation of the East End, from the era of segregation and the vibrant life of Black-owned businesses to the impacts of urban renewal, public housing developments, and recent gentrification. Today, systemic barriers mean much of this living memory is at risk of disappearing without a trust-first, offline-ready channel designed specifically for their needs.

### Who this work centers — “Users B3: The Longtime Resident with Neighborhood Stories”
This initiative centers on a 75-year-old African American woman from Church Hill. She has watched her neighborhood change dramatically and holds the histories of local networks, community events, and neighborhood institutions that are rarely captured in official public records. She possesses a wealth of knowledge but currently has no trusted, low-barrier channel to share it, often wondering if her everyday stories even matter to the broader historical narrative.

## Neighborhood Context and Trusted Anchors — Where trust already lives
To reach this user, engagement must happen where she already feels safe and valued. The Market @ 25th, a 27,000-square-foot grocery store that opened in April 2019, was specifically designed as a community anchor for Church Hill [2] [3]. The store integrates local identity directly into its environment, featuring aisle signs named after neighborhood churches and graphic designs honoring local schools and neighborhoods like Armstrong, Chimborazo, Fairfield, George Mason, and Woodville [3]. 

Local elder leaders, such as Mary White Thompson (affectionately known as the "Mayor of 22nd Street"), championed the creation of this supermarket through decades of civic advocacy [2]. Co-locating story capture at these proven, visible anchors raises participation and legitimacy. *[Inference: Partnering with adjacent East End institutions with long service footprints, such as the Peter Paul Development Center and the Robinson Theater, will further embed the project in trusted community networks.]*

## Access Reality Check — Broadband, smartphones, age, race, and income in the East End
The digital divide in Richmond's East End is acute and heavily amplified by age and race. Relying on web-based portals or smartphone apps will actively exclude the target demographic.

* **Broadband and Smartphone Gaps**: In census tract 204 (where I-64 and I-95 intersect, covering Mosby, Brauers, Fairmount, Whitcomb, and Upper Shockoe Valley), a staggering **66.2% of households lack broadband internet access** and **47.7% lack smartphone access** [1]. Adjacent tracts (301, 202, 201, 207) covering Eastview, Fairfield, Creighton, Woodville, Peter Paul, Church Hill North, Union Hill, and Gilpin also show the highest shares of disconnected households [1].
* **Age Disparities**: Richmond residents aged 55 and older are **73% more likely not to have internet access** than younger cohorts [1]. Nationally, only 61% of adults 65 and older own a smartphone [4].
* **Racial Disparities**: Black or African American Richmond residents disproportionately experience barriers across all technologies. They are **115% more likely to lack internet access**, **55% more likely to lack cell data**, and **138% more likely to lack a bank account** compared to the city population as a whole [1]. 
* **Cellular Reliance**: Minority respondents in Virginia are more likely to access the internet at home using a cellular data plan (46% compared to 41% for non-minorities) [5].

**Implication**: Design must default to voice, in-person, and paper channels. Digital tools must be strictly optional.

## Language Landscape and City Support — Inclusion without shifting the core
While the primary persona is an English-speaking African American elder, the broader East End community is increasingly multilingual. In Richmond city, 12.1% of persons aged 5 years and older speak a language other than English at home [6]. In the Richmond City (South & East) PUMA, which includes Church Hill, **16.5% of households report speaking a non-English language at home**, with Spanish being the most common (13.7%), followed by French and Afro-Asiatic languages like Amharic and Somali [7].

The City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) and the iSpeak Richmond initiative provide free interpretation and translation services to ensure equitable access [8] [9]. 
**Action**: To widen household participation (including neighbors and caregivers) without shifting focus from the primary English-speaking elder persona, the project should provide Spanish-forward flyers, a Spanish IVR option on the phone hotline, and coordinate with OIRE for overflow interpretation needs.

## Trust, Safety, and Governance — From extractive to co-authored
Fear of scams and past bureaucratic harms create a high bar for trust among older adults. Nationally, one in five aging individuals is affected by cybercriminals, representing over **$2.56 billion in total annual losses** [10]. This higher-than-average targeting leads to caution, skepticism, and fear of the internet [10]. Furthermore, Richmond has a history of language-access litigation and code-enforcement tensions that heighten wariness of official city data collection [11].

To prevent the project from feeling extractive, transparent consent and community oversight are non-negotiable. 
* **Action**: Use recognizable local caller IDs and avoid sending digital links. Anchor every touchpoint in familiar institutions.
* **Action**: Lead with a plain-language, one-page "what will happen with my story" handout.
* **Action**: Establish a community oversight panel (including at least two Church Hill elders) with veto power on story use beyond the original scope. Implement granular contributor permissions (public, community-only, archive-only, private).

## Channel and Feature Design — What will actually work here
To successfully reach this user, the project must lead with human-supported, phone-first, and place-based capture, layering robust consent and tangible returns.

### Channel–Barrier Fit Matrix

| Channel | Access Need | Strengths | Risks | Mitigations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Local 804 hotline (voicemail + live callback)** | Any phone | Lowest barrier; private; schedule-friendly | Robocall fatigue; mistrust of unknown numbers | Use recognizable caller ID; publish number on church bulletins and Market @ 25th signage |
| **In-person story booth at Market @ 25th/events** | Physical presence | High trust; social proof; instant help | Mobility limitations; limited hours | Align with grocery peak times; provide ADA seating; offer transport vouchers |
| **Home-visit recordings via partners** | Appointment | Reaches mobility-limited elders; builds deep rapport | Staffing and logistical costs | *[Inference: Train volunteers from Peter Paul; batch visits per block]* |
| **Paper mail-in story kits (prepaid return)** | Mail access | Familiar format; requires no technology | Slower process; handwriting strain | Offer a call-in option to "dictate" responses; include large-print forms |
| **Optional web page/audio upload** | Smartphone/computer | Good for caregivers/grandkids assisting elders | Excludes the core user if made mandatory | Make strictly optional; ensure sub-5MB page sizes; require no logins |

### Must-Have Features
* **Plain-language consent**: Selectable visibility options (public, community-only, archive-only, private).
* **"Return to giver" artifacts**: Provide a printed summary, CD, or USB drive of their story so they have a tangible keepsake.
* **Staffed assistance**: Navigators available at every touchpoint to guide the process.

## Incentives and Logistics — Pay, scheduling, and transport that fit reality
Because Black Richmond residents are **138% more likely to be unbanked** and **93% more likely to lack credit/debit cards** [1], digital or bank-dependent incentives will fail. Honoraria must be immediate and cash-equivalent. Furthermore, scheduling should align with existing routines, such as post-church Sunday afternoons or peak grocery shopping times.

### Incentive Options vs Access Fit

| Option | Banking Required | Immediate | Friction | Fit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Grocery gift card (Market @ 25th)** | No | Yes | Low | **Strong** |
| **Cash (stipend at event)** | No | Yes | Low | **Strong** |
| **Paper check** | Yes (to cash cheaply) | No | Medium | Weak |
| **Prepaid Visa** | Sometimes (online activation) | Sometimes | Medium | Mixed |
| **Digital transfer (Zelle/Venmo)** | Yes (plus smartphone) | No | High | Poor |

## Risk and Failure Modes — What will fail this user and why
Designing a standard modern tech solution will reliably exclude the core persona. The following features must be avoided:
* **App-first or Web-only portals**: With up to 66.2% of key East End tracts lacking broadband and 47.7% lacking smartphones [1], "self-serve" digital tools will fail.
* **Typed narratives required**: Vision and dexterity barriers, combined with "not a good writer" self-censorship, will suppress participation.
* **Bank-only payments**: Direct deposits or Venmo transfers will exclude the highly unbanked Black senior population [1].
* **Anonymous corporate branding**: Given the high prevalence of senior cybercrime [10], tools lacking local, recognizable institutional anchors will be rejected as potential scams.

## Pilot Plan — 90-day proof with measurable learning
A 90-day, three-site pilot will validate the channel mix, consent user experience, and incentive effectiveness.
1. **Setup**: Stand up the local 804 hotline, train community navigators, and print Spanish/English flyers (coordinating with OIRE for language support).
2. **Deployment**: Host 6 pop-up "story hours" at The Market @ 25th, 4 Sunday sessions at a partner church, and 3 days of batched home-visits.
3. **Archiving**: Establish the pipeline to VCU Libraries and The Valentine, ensuring contributor permission settings dictate access levels.

## Measurement and Learning — Proving reach, trust, and quality
Success must be measured by inclusion and agency, not just raw data counts.
* **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**: 
 * Number of unique elder contributors (65+).
 * Share of participants from highly disconnected East End tracts (201, 202, 204, 207, 301).
 * Share of users choosing each consent level (indicating trust in the system).
 * Completion rate of "return-to-giver" artifacts (CDs/printed summaries delivered).
 * Incentive redemption rates.
 * Trust survey results (e.g., agreement with "I felt respected" and "I understand how my story will be used").

## Equity and Urgency Case — Why this user is the priority
The knowledge held by Church Hill's longtime Black residents is irreplaceable and highly time-sensitive. The barriers to capturing this history are highest exactly where the history is richest. Prioritizing this demographic advances equity by correcting historic under-recording and mitigates imminent memory loss amidst rapid neighborhood gentrification and demographic shifts. If we do not build tools that meet them where they are today, their stories will be permanently lost.
