# Problem Landscape: Neighborhood Change in Richmond, Virginia

## Executive Summary

Richmond, Virginia, is currently navigating a complex landscape of neighborhood change, driven by a combination of historical displacement, market-driven gentrification, and modern urban redevelopment policies. This document synthesizes the forces reshaping Richmond’s neighborhoods, providing evidence-based annotations and actionable takeaways for policymakers and community stakeholders. 

Key findings indicate that historical infrastructure projects, such as the 1955 construction of the Richmond-Petersburg Turnpike (I-95) through Jackson Ward, established a precedent of displacement that continues to impact wealth distribution today [1] [2]. Subsequent urban renewal efforts, notably the 1970 Fulton project, further eroded historic Black communities [3] [4]. Today, the city attempts to balance growth and equity through the Richmond 300 Master Plan [5], while managing the active redevelopment of public housing like Creighton Court [6] [7] and combating market-driven displacement in neighborhoods like Church Hill [8]. To prevent the repetition of past public-private failures, such as the 6th Street Marketplace [9] [10], future interventions must mandate community benefit agreements and strict displacement-mitigation metrics.

## 1. Historical Displacement – Jackson Ward & the I-95 Turnpike

The contemporary wealth and housing gaps in Richmond cannot be understood without analyzing mid-20th-century infrastructure projects that targeted thriving minority neighborhoods. 

**The Event:** Beginning in 1955, the construction of the Richmond-Petersburg Turnpike (which later became Interstate 95) sliced directly through Jackson Ward [1] [2]. Known historically as the "Black Wall Street," Jackson Ward was the crown jewel of Black Richmond, featuring thriving businesses, cultural institutions, and income diversity [11] [12]. 

**The Impact:** The highway's construction permanently divided the community in two [1]. This eminent domain action displaced more than 1,000 residents and business owners [2]. The loss of concentrated Black economic power created a long-term wealth gap that still surfaces in today’s eviction and gentrification data.

**Strategic Action:** City-wide equity audits should weight historic loss metrics (e.g., displaced households) when allocating funds under the Richmond 300 master plan. 
*(Reference: Library of Virginia exhibition "House to Highway: Reclaiming a Community History" - https://www.lva.virginia.gov/events/exhibitions/house-to-highway)*

## 2. Urban Renewal "Fulton" Project – 1970 Plan

Following the highway construction, federal urban renewal policies facilitated further displacement in Richmond's East End.

**The Event:** The Fulton Urban Renewal Plan was unveiled and adopted in 1970 [3]. This federally-funded initiative targeted Historic Fulton, a predominantly Black neighborhood [4]. 

**The Impact:** The project resulted in the total demolition of the historic community [13] [4]. Widely considered a failure due to the lack of timely development, the promised replacement housing and commercial revitalization never materialized at scale, leaving the area permanently altered and largely underdeveloped [13]. 

**Strategic Action:** Fulton serves as a cautionary template for contemporary mixed-income projects. Any future redevelopment in Fulton-adjacent zones must include legally binding right-to-return guarantees and phased construction to avoid "ghost-development."
*(Reference: Historic Fulton Oral History Project - https://scholarscompass.vcu.edu/ful/)*

## 5. Market-Driven Gentrification – Church Hill & Jackson Ward

While historical displacement was driven by government action, modern displacement is largely driven by private market forces.

**The Event:** Neighborhoods such as Jackson Ward and Church Hill have experienced significant gentrification [8]. Private developers have capitalized on historic housing stock, leading to rapid property value appreciation.

**The Impact:** This market-driven gentrification creates severe displacement pressure on legacy residents, particularly renters and low-income homeowners [8]. The pattern mirrors earlier urban renewal displacements but is driven by economics rather than federal mandates, making regulatory response more complex.

**Strategic Action:** Adopt a Community Benefit Agreement (CBA) requirement for any large-scale private development project in Church Hill or Jackson Ward, mandating on-site affordable units and local hiring quotas to ensure wealth generation remains within the community.

## 6. Public-Private Failure – 6th Street Marketplace

When attempting to revitalize downtown spaces, Richmond has a history of ambitious but ultimately unsuccessful public-private partnerships.

**The Event:** The 6th Street Marketplace was developed as a public-private partnership between the city and The Rouse Co. [10]. 

**The Impact:** The project invested millions of dollars but is widely recognized as a failed project [9]. It serves as a concrete illustration that large-scale commercial infill without deep community integration and sustainable economic modeling often fails, draining municipal resources.

**Strategic Action:** Conduct a formal post-mortem review of the 6th Street Marketplace failure to establish strict viability criteria for future public-private partnerships, reallocating high-risk commercial subsidies toward small-business micro-grants in adjacent neighborhoods.
