# Richmond’s Rapid Neighborhood Shift: Capturing Community Memory Before It’s Gone

## Executive Summary

Richmond is undergoing a period of hyper-accelerated physical and demographic transformation, creating a rapidly closing window to capture the city's neighborhood memory. Corridor-scale redevelopment along the Ashe–I-95 spine, anchored by the 67.57-acre Diamond District and the April 2026 opening of CarMax Park [1] [2], is fundamentally altering the city's landscape. Simultaneously, policy-enabled upzoning in Greater Scott's Addition and the City Center Innovation District is unlocking unlimited height and density, foreshadowing massive tenant and use turnover [3] [4]. 

This physical redevelopment is mirrored by severe demographic shifts in legacy neighborhoods. In Church Hill, the "pressure of displacement" has already driven a 23% decline in Black homeownership while white homeownership surged 159% [5] [6]. Crucial community gathering places, such as Manchester's Brewer's Café and Brewer's Waffles, have already succumbed to financial strain and eviction [7] [8]. While robust oral history projects exist for Jackson Ward and university campuses [9] [10], massive gaps remain in Southside, Scott's Addition, and Church Hill North. A civic story collection tool is not an optional heritage project; it is a time-sensitive intervention required immediately to document informal histories, at-risk businesses, and legacy social networks before the next wave of demolition permits and lease renewals erases them entirely.

## Why This Matters Now — 2026 ushers in irreversible, corridor-wide change

The convergence of massive public-private infrastructure projects and aggressive rezoning policies has created a short window—measured in months, not years—to capture pre-redevelopment memory.

### Diamond District Phase 1 deadlines compress the capture window
The 67.57-acre Diamond District redevelopment is slated to bring 2,300 to 3,200 residential units and up to 4,800 new residents over a 15-year build-out [1]. However, the immediate urgency is driven by Phase 1: the opening of CarMax Park, the new home for the Richmond Flying Squirrels, in April 2026 [2]. This opening represents a hard pivot from legacy uses to entertainment district patterns. Early entertainment and retail mixes inevitably displace informal vendors and repurpose parking lots, erasing "in-between" uses that are rarely captured in formal city archives. 

### Policy signals unlock height and density across adjacent districts
Policy-enabled upzoning serves as a leading indicator of cultural turnover. The City Center Innovation District plan, adopted by the City Council in January 2022, calls for rezoning to allow mixed uses, ground floor activation, and unlimited height and density [3]. Similarly, the 2021 rezoning of Greater Scott's Addition supports mid-to-high-rise development, with guidelines setting minimum five-story typologies [11] [4]. These policy shifts act as "go" signals for developers, meaning the time to document older industrial cultures, working-class social clubs, and maker spaces is right now, before demolition or conversion begins.

Different neighborhoods face distinct risks based on their unique development pipelines and demographic vulnerabilities. Church Hill, Manchester, Greater Scott's Addition, and the City Center periphery form the top-tier focus for immediate memory capture.

## What’s at Risk — The specific community knowledge likely to be lost

The most perishable assets in Richmond's changing landscape are informal and place-based. As property values rise, the physical spaces that hold community memory are the first to disappear.

### Businesses and Third Places lose continuity first
Community gathering places are high-leverage story hubs but are financially vulnerable. In Manchester, Brewer's Waffles closed in March 2021 due to pandemic-induced foot traffic reductions [8], followed by the closure of its sibling business, Brewer's Café, in October 2022 after a move to a larger Hull Street storefront led to financial difficulties and eviction notices [7]. Conversely, Northside’s Brookland Park demonstrates that Black-owned barbershops, salons, and restaurants can successfully anchor cultural presence during demographic shifts, serving as vital gathering spaces to discuss politics and culture [6]. 

### Institutions and civic memory under redevelopment pressure
Redevelopment often threatens the continuity of civic organizing and institutional memory. The City Center Innovation District plans to reduce the municipal footprint by redeveloping vacant city-owned properties and relocating city functions [3]. Furthermore, historical scholarship on the Navy Hill redevelopment highlights how community-based disruption and organized resistance successfully politicized growth-oriented development rhetoric [14]. Recording these organizing tactics and civic debates now can inform more inclusive choices in future City Center and Diamond District phases.

### Industrial and labor stories in transition zones
Greater Scott's Addition is undergoing a massive shift from an industrial hub to a mixed-use neighborhood. In 2019, approximately 458 acres (60% of the area's total land) were vacant or underdeveloped [13]. As this land is absorbed by new housing and employment developments, the stories of warehouse workers, early makers, and the foundational years of the neighborhood's brewery boom are at severe risk of being overwritten.

## How Urgent Is “Urgent”? — A milestone-driven capture timeline

The window for capture is tiered, requiring immediate action for stadium-adjacent areas and sustained engagement for broader policy-driven redevelopment zones.

### 0–3 months: Stadium pre-opening and launch
CarMax Park opens in April 2026 [2]. This requires an immediate sprint to photo- and audio-document pre-game informal uses, interview legacy workers from The Diamond era, and capture the stories of neighborhood residents before game-day traffic and new retail fundamentally alter the environment.

### 3–12 months: Early retail and tenant turnover
As Phase 1 of the Diamond District comes online, spillover effects will hit Greater Scott's Addition and Manchester. Mobile clinics should be deployed to barbershops and salons, focusing on renters facing aggressive cash-offer solicitations and code enforcement pressures—tactics that historically drive preemptive displacement in areas like Church Hill [5].

### 12–24 months: Policy-to-project conversion around City Center
As the City Center Innovation District moves from rezoning to active RFPs and municipal relocations [3], partnerships with civic groups must be activated to record the impacts of reconfiguration. Tracking demolition permits will be essential to trigger micro-collections before physical landmarks are razed.

## Oral History Ecosystem — Who’s capturing what, and gaps

Richmond has a rich but geographically uneven oral history ecosystem. A new civic story collection tool must complement, rather than duplicate, existing efforts.

### Existing efforts and institutional strengths
Jackson Ward is well-supported by dedicated initiatives. The Ealey Project, in partnership with the Library of Virginia, is actively preserving the legacy of civil rights attorney Roland "Duke" Ealey and conducting oral histories regarding Jackson Ward's post-Reconstruction and Civil Rights eras [10]. The Black History Museum and Cultural Center of Virginia is also physically anchored in Jackson Ward [15]. Additionally, the University of Richmond's Race & Racism Project conducts oral histories focusing on marginalized alumni and staff experiences [9]. 

### Coverage gaps to fill
Despite these strong institutional efforts, there is limited systematic coverage of Southside (Manchester), the industrial-to-mixed-use transitions in Scott’s Addition, and the rapidly gentrifying networks of Church Hill North. The civic story tool must prioritize these specific geographies, utilizing mobile collection clinics and pop-ups to reach populations outside the traditional museum and university spheres.

## Deployment Strategy for a Civic Story Collection Tool — Fast, place-based, and partnership-driven

To succeed, the collection tool must meet people where memory lives—in third places and at milestone events—and co-own the outputs with trusted community institutions.

### 90-day sprint: Stadium corridor memory rescue
Launch immediate pop-ups at the ballpark vicinity, Carver/Ashe Boulevard, and legacy Diamond tailgate areas. This sprint must focus on rapid processing and public sharing to build momentum and demonstrate immediate value to the community.

### Neighborhood partnerships that unlock trust and access

| Partner Target | Asset / Advantage | Gap it fills | Role in collection |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Black-owned barbershops/salons** (Brookland Park, Hull St.) | Trusted community hubs | Reaches multi-generational networks | Host story booths; recruit narrators |
| **Churches/faith orgs** (Church Hill North, Manchester) | Access to elders, family archives | Under-documented ministries | Host "Memory Sundays"; scan-and-save events |
| **Small business associations** (Scott’s Addition, Hull St.) | Deep merchant histories | Commercial turnover risk | Facilitate after-hours shop interviews |
| **Museums/archives** (Black History Museum; Library of VA) | Preservation expertise | Long-term storage and access | Co-archive MOUs; provide training |
| **City engagement channels** (Diamond/City Center teams) | Large audience reach | Broad story recruitment | Cross-promotion; event co-location |

### Data, consent, and preservation
All collected stories must utilize community licenses with immediate dual deposit (one copy to the community organization, one to a public repository). Ethic-of-care protocols must be strictly enforced for sensitive topics, particularly when discussing displacement, eviction, or code enforcement pressure.

## Risks and Mitigations — Getting ahead of “but what about…”

Balancing speed with rigor and community trust is paramount. The following risks must be proactively managed:

* **Bias toward “newcomer stories”**: Rapid collection in gentrifying areas often over-samples new, affluent residents. **Mitigation**: Establish strict quota targets for elders and longtime residents, relying heavily on church-based recruitment.
* **Over-attribution of closures to gentrification**: Assuming all business closures (e.g., Brewer’s Café) are solely due to rent spikes ignores nuance. **Mitigation**: Conduct context interviews that capture multi-causality, including relocation logistics, public controversies, and pandemic hangovers [7] [8].
* **Re-traumatization**: Discussing displacement can be deeply painful. **Mitigation**: Implement trauma-informed interviewing techniques, clear opt-out controls, and mandatory community review of published materials.
* **Shelf life risk**: Digital stories can easily be lost to link rot. **Mitigation**: Pre-arrange preservation partnerships with the Library of Virginia and the Black History Museum, utilizing open formats and strict metadata standards.
