# Win the First 15 Seconds: Richmond-Proven Framing That Converts Skeptical Civic Judges

## Executive Summary
In 90-second civic tech demos, the opening 15 seconds dictate whether judges view a team as credible partners or naive technologists. Our research indicates that replacing rounded estimates (e.g., "200+ events") with verifiable, hyper-local anchors significantly increases persuasiveness. By leveraging documented figures—such as the 4,858 events hosted by the Richmond Performing Arts Alliance (RPAA) over a decade [1], the 200,000 attendees at the Richmond Folk Festival [2], and the approximately 70 galleries across the region [3] —teams can establish immediate authority. 

Furthermore, positioning must respect existing ecosystems. Framing an arts tool as making existing organizations "collectively visible" rather than claiming to be a "one-stop shop" prevents defensive reactions from community stakeholders. Crucially, teams must avoid "AI-first" introductions; the City of Richmond's own Grant Procedures Manual explicitly notes there is "no official policy or clarity on the use of AI" [4], signaling a cautious municipal environment. The winning strategy relies on neighborhood specificity, additive positioning, and modest, measurable impact claims.

## Why Openings Decide 90-Second Demos — Anchor in Richmond facts, not abstractions
Opening lines matter disproportionately because judges form rapid impressions. A weak, generic opening creates a credibility deficit that the rest of the demo must overcome. Leading with one Richmond-specific, cited number paired with a relatable personal pain point earns judge trust immediately. When you anchor your narrative in verifiable facts rather than abstractions, you signal that you have done the groundwork to understand the community's actual landscape.

### Evidence Judges Believe — RPAA, Folk Festival, 70 galleries
Using verifiable local anchors beats rounded estimates every time. While the Virginia Museum of Fine Arts (VMFA) operates at a massive scale, their financial statements do not publish a clean "events count" metric [5] [6]. Instead, we can rely on highly specific data from the RPAA, Venture Richmond, and Visit Richmond to build a defensible floor for arts volume. 

| Source | Metric & Period | Strategic Value for Demo |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Richmond Performing Arts Alliance (RPAA)** | **4,858 performances, rehearsals, and events** over 10 years [1] | Provides a verifiable baseline of ~486 events/year (~40/month) at just two flagship venues (Altria Theater and Dominion Energy Center). |
| **Richmond Folk Festival** | **200,000 people** over a three-day weekend [2] [7] | Acts as powerful social proof of Richmond's massive appetite for cultural programming and arts engagement. |
| **Visit Richmond** | **About 70 galleries** in the region [3] | Highlights the fragmented nature of the arts scene, proving that discovery is a genuine logistical challenge for residents. |

*Takeaway: Pair the massive 200,000-attendance festival stat with the RPAA monthly baseline to avoid overgeneralization. Say: "On one weekend, 200,000 people; on a typical month, ~40 events at just two venues."*

## Framing Language Guide — Scripts that respect partners and persuade skeptics
The core principle of civic tech framing is to state exactly what the tool actually does (aggregate, visualize, complement) rather than what it aspires to own. 

### Opening Lines: Two Options Each, With "When to Use"
**Arts Discovery (Data-Led)**
*Script:* "At just two venues—Altria Theater and Dominion Energy Center—Richmond stages about 40 performances a month. Add 70 galleries and neighborhood theaters, and most residents still find only a fraction—because discovery lives across dozens of sites and feeds."
*When to use:* Use when you can display source logos and footnotes live on-screen to project rigorous research.

**Arts Discovery (Narrative-Led)**
*Script:* "On First Fridays in Jackson Ward, galleries spill onto Broad Street. But the rest of the month, discovery depends on who you follow and what a friend happens to mention."
*When to use:* Use when presenting to a storytelling-first panel; it grounds the problem in a recognizable Richmond cultural institution before pivoting to the fragmentation pain point.

**Story Collection (Data-Led)**
*Script:* "Major plans steer billions, but our public input skews to hearings and forms. There is no Richmond channel for long-memory neighborhood stories that planners can actually search and reuse."
*When to use:* Use when city planners or municipal staff are present. Avoid implying the City has already endorsed this.

**Story Collection (Narrative-Led)**
*Script:* "In Church Hill and Manchester, longtime neighbors can tell you what was here before—and what mattered. When they are gone, that knowledge goes with them, and plans proceed without it."
*When to use:* Use when community organizers or arts judges are on the panel to emphasize the irreversible loss of cultural memory.

### Positioning Statements: Scope and Relationship to Existing Systems
**Arts Discovery:** "We are not replacing anyone's website or Instagram—we are making them collectively visible. Artists post where they already post; our tool assembles Richmond's arts into one browsable map and feed."

**Story Collection:** "We give residents a dedicated space for place-based stories, and give City staff one more qualitative signal—complementing surveys and hearings with context those processes miss."

### Closing Impact Statements: Honest, Specific, Measurable
**Arts Discovery:** "Small and mid-sized organizations gain visibility without extra admin. Over time, we map where arts activity concentrates—and where it is thin—so partners can target outreach."

**Story Collection:** "Planners get searchable, geotagged narratives next to parcels and plan elements. The goal isn't to automate policy—it is to make invisible knowledge visible."

### Phrases to Avoid and Why
Certain buzzwords trigger immediate skepticism from judges who have seen civic tech tools overpromise and underdeliver. 

| Overclaim / Risky Phrase | Why it Backfires | Safer Alternative |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "One-stop shop," "definitive," or "official" | Implies a level of completeness and municipal authority you do not own, creating unsupportable accountability. | "Collectively visible," "brought together," "aggregated feed." |
| "AI-powered platform" (in the opening) | Tech-first framing triggers skepticism. The City of Richmond notes there is "no official policy or clarity on the use of AI" [4]. | "Finds what artists already publish," "reduces administrative burden." |
| "Replace public input" | Threatens the legitimacy of existing, legally mandated civic processes. | "Complement existing engagement," "add a new qualitative signal." |

*Takeaway: Keep AI under the hood. If asked about AI, describe it strictly as a utility to reduce administrative burden, not as the headline feature.*

## Known Gaps and How We'll Close Them Fast — De-risking questions judges will ask
Pre-empt judge skepticism by acknowledging what you don't know and showing a two-week plan to shore up data and endorsements.

**Unverified Today:**
* Official City planning documents (e.g., Richmond 300) explicitly acknowledging limitations in community engagement.
* Public statements from City Council or Planning staff explicitly endorsing narrative-input tools.

**2-Week Action Plan:**
* Scrape and tally 60 days of Eventbrite and Instagram tags (#rvaarts, #rvamusic) to produce a defendable "events/month" floor with a clear methodology.
* Request monthly event counts or "programs/year" from VMFA, VCUarts, and RPAA via their communications offices.
* Ask the Department of Planning and Development Review (PDR) for a neutral quote on the value of qualitative stories, ensuring it does not imply official endorsement.
* Build a coverage heatmap and a "known gaps" list, committing to a community submission form for DIY and pop-up events.

## Risk and Failure Modes — What to avoid and how
Most demo failures stem from overclaiming coverage or leading with technology. Teams that assert their tool is the "definitive calendar" inevitably get pinned on coverage gaps and maintenance burdens. 

**Risks:**
* Claiming an "official calendar" invites immediate pushback from existing arts organizations.
* An "AI-first" opener increases scrutiny and distracts from the community problem.
* Using festival attendance as a proxy for daily volume misrepresents the baseline reality.

**Mitigations:**
* Use scope qualifiers like "at least" and "at just these sources" alongside footnotes.
* Include a live slide showing indexed sources (e.g., CultureWorks, Eventbrite) and a "what we'll add next" backlog to prove a partnership mindset.

## Metrics That Matter in Pilots — Modest, provable wins
Judges reward honest limits. Close your demo by committing to modest, measurable impacts rather than vanity metrics.

**Arts Discovery:**
* Percentage increase in click-through rates from the aggregated feed to small organization event pages (baseline vs. 4-week pilot).
* Percentage of events auto-detected versus manually submitted, alongside median time-to-publish.

**Story Collection:**
* Number of geotagged stories successfully linked to specific plan elements or parcels.
* Staff reuse rate: The number of resident stories cited in internal municipal memos or briefs.
