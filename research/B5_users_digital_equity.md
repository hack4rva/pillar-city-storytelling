# B5 Users Digital Equity – Research Brief

*Version: 2026-03*

## Executive Summary
The digital-equity analysis for B5 users reveals three dominant user typologies based on technology-related barriers: **Non-Users**, **Conditional Users**, and **Full Users** [1]. While national policies such as the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) Program have allocated $42.45 billion to expand high-speed internet access through planning, infrastructure deployment, and adoption programs across all 50 states [2], uneven rollout and low-income digital-skill gaps persist. Furthermore, the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) invests roughly $65 billion to support broadband deployment and adoption to promote digital equity [3]. 

Key takeaways:
* **Non-Users** lack reliable internet access, often due to geographic isolation or unaffordable service plans.
* **Conditional Users** have connectivity but experience intermittent service, outdated devices, or limited digital literacy [1].
* **Full Users** enjoy robust access but still report sub-optimal usage due to the affordability of high-speed plans.

**Action:** Prioritize infrastructure subsidies for the non-user cohort, pair them with device-grant programs, and embed community-led digital-literacy curricula for conditional users.

## Key Access Barriers

1. **Infrastructure Deficits:** Significant portions of rural and underserved tracts still lack providers offering adequate broadband speeds.
2. **Affordability:** High-speed internet costs remain a significant burden for low-income households, limiting adoption even where infrastructure exists.
3. **Device Obsolescence:** Conditional users frequently rely on outdated devices, limiting compatibility with modern web standards and digital services [1].
4. **Digital Literacy Gaps:** A lack of digital skills remains a primary reason for not adopting broadband among non-users.
5. **Policy Fragmentation:** Multiple federal, state, and local initiatives operate simultaneously, requiring coordinated implementation strategies. For example, the California Digital Equity Plan outlines seven key activities to realize the vision of Broadband for All [4].
