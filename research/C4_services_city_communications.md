# From Silos to Stories: Unifying Richmond's Arts Communications

## Executive Summary

The City of Richmond possesses powerful distribution channels and well-funded civic engagement frameworks, but its arts and culture communications remain highly fragmented. Currently, residents must navigate a maze of department-specific pages, off-domain survey tools, and disjointed newsletters to discover cultural events or participate in civic storytelling. 

**Key Strategic Insights:**
* **OSC is the natural "front door" for arts storytelling:** The Office of Strategic Communications (OSC) already manages the City's official social media, the *Hey, Richmond* newsletter, and the Poet Laureate program [1] [2]. It is uniquely positioned to host a unified arts aggregator.
* **Arts content is siloed across departments:** The Public Art Commission (PAC) manages a 1% allocation for art on capital projects over $250,000 [3], while Parks & Recreation handles community facilities [4]. There is no centralized calendar or discovery hub on rva.gov.
* **311 is a high-traffic, untapped distribution surface:** RVA311 supported over 83,000 requests in 2024 across more than 20 City agencies [5]. While currently used for service tickets, its web and app interfaces offer prime real estate for context-aware arts promotion.
* **Participatory Budgeting (PB) is an engagement-ready infrastructure:** The Richmond People's Budget (RVAPB) operates with a $550,000 operations budget and a $3 million Cycle 1 implementation budget [6]. Its rulebook explicitly mandates the inclusion of art, culture, and bilingual materials (English/Spanish/ASL) in community spaces [6].
* **Engagement tools are proliferating off-domain:** The City relies heavily on external platforms like Eventbrite, SurveyPlanet, ArcGIS, and standalone sites (rvapb.org, richmondinspire.com) [7] [4] [8] [9]. This creates data silos and risks eroding trust due to a lack of canonical rva.gov landing pages.
* **Champion identification requires verification:** While Chaya Braxton was named as a potential champion, current OSC directories list other Public Information Officers (PIOs) for key departments (e.g., Michael Hinkle for Planning, Nykita Doggette for General Services) [10]. Her specific remit must be confirmed to establish a clear product owner.

## Purpose and Decision Frame

This report investigates how the City of Richmond currently communicates about arts, culture, and community engagement, and provides a blueprint for unifying these efforts. Richmond has strong distribution primitives—OSC's media reach, RVA311's high-volume traffic, and RVAPB's grassroots infrastructure—but lacks a cohesive arts surface. The fastest path to scale is establishing a unified, OSC-led hub that integrates feeds from Planning, Parks & Recreation, and civic engagement platforms.

## How Richmond Communicates Arts Today

Richmond's current arts communication strategy relies on strong but isolated storytelling assets. The Public Art Commission (PAC) successfully mandates a 1% allocation for art on appropriate capital projects over $250,000 and maintains a public art project map [3]. However, residents must subscribe to a separate "Richmond 300" Mailchimp newsletter to stay informed about PAC projects [3]. 

Similarly, the Poet Laureate program, housed within OSC, is a powerful storytelling engine. The current Laureate, Joanna Lee, regularly publishes in the *Hey, Richmond* newsletter to "bear witness to the times" and serve as a voice for residents [2] [11]. Meanwhile, Parks & Recreation drives engagement through standalone initiatives like the "Richmond INSPIRE" vision plan, hosting workshops across all council districts [4]. Despite these strengths, there is no single discovery surface or cross-department calendar on rva.gov, forcing residents to hunt for cultural touchpoints across different departmental silos.

## Civic Engagement Stack: PB, 311, and Surveys as Culture Amplifiers

Richmond's existing civic engagement tools can double as two-way arts connectors if lightly re-tooled.

### Participatory Budgeting Embeds Arts-Friendly Engagement
The Richmond People's Budget (RVAPB) is a highly structured, well-funded engagement vehicle. Operating on a two-year cycle with a $550,000 operations budget and a $3 million implementation budget for Cycle 1, RVAPB is designed to reach marginalized communities [6]. Crucially, the RVAPB rulebook explicitly lists "Include art & culture, food, childcare and fun to make spaces more accessible and inviting" as a core community building goal [6]. It also mandates that all materials and events be available in English, Spanish, and ASL [6]. Integrating the PB calendar and idea map into a central arts hub would normalize civic arts participation.

### RVA311 Reach is an Untapped Promo Layer
RVA311 is a high-traffic portal, partnering with over 30 different departments and handling over 83,000 requests in 2024 [5]. While it currently focuses on non-emergency infrastructure and social services (potholes, taxes, bulk waste) [5], its web portal and mobile app offer prime real estate. The City could pilot context-aware, opt-in arts alerts or geo-targeted "What's On Near You" banners within the 311 interface to convert service-seekers into cultural participants.

### Current Survey Ecosystem is Siloed
The City frequently uses off-domain tools for public input. Budget town halls utilize Eventbrite for registration and SurveyPlanet for prioritization [8]. The Department of Public Works uses ArcGIS for intersection safety surveys [9], and Parks & Recreation uses a standalone site (richmondinspire.com) [4]. This fragmentation prevents the City from building a unified audience profile. Standardizing UTM tags, implementing common metadata, and requiring canonical rva.gov landing pages for all external tools will solve this data fragmentation.

## Gaps in City-Facilitated Arts Discovery

The primary gap in Richmond's arts discovery is the absence of a unified, bilingual, accessible calendar. Because content is scattered across Planning, Parks & Recreation, and OSC, residents suffer from low discoverability. Specific gaps include:
* **No Central Calendar:** Arts events are not aggregated in one place.
* **Off-Domain Drift:** Key initiatives live on WordPress (rvapb.org) or standalone URLs (richmondinspire.com) [7] [4].
* **Inconsistent Language Parity:** While PB mandates English, Spanish, and ASL [6], the main rva.gov site relies heavily on a generic Google Translate widget and phone-based translation requests [5].
* **Lack of Syndication:** There are no open RSS/JSON feeds or schema.org/Event markups to allow community partners to auto-ingest City arts data.

## Gaps in City-Facilitated Civic Storytelling

Currently, the City primarily publishes *to* residents rather than *with* them. The Poet Laureate program is a strong success anchor; the Laureate receives a $5,000 annual stipend (up from $4,000) and $2,000 in project development funds to implement community-based poetry projects [2] [11]. However, beyond the Laureate, there is no structured intake pipeline for resident-submitted stories. The City lacks a moderated "Share Your Story" portal, resulting in limited neighborhood arts spotlights and weak archiving of grassroots cultural narratives.

## Recommendation: The Most Natural Home for an Arts Aggregator

**The Office of Strategic Communications (OSC) is the most pragmatic and natural home for a unified arts aggregator and story portal.** 

While Parks & Recreation manages venues [4] and the Office of Neighborhood Engagement connects with communities [12], OSC already possesses the editorial capacity, the PIO network, the *Hey, Richmond* newsletter, and the social media footprint required to drive traffic [10] [1]. Furthermore, OSC already houses the Poet Laureate program, proving its capability to manage cultural assets [2]. 

Creating a standalone microsite would only add another silo. Instead, the City should launch `rva.gov/arts` as an OSC-managed hub that ingests automated feeds from PAC and PRCF, while utilizing RVA311 and RVAPB to supply reach and participation.

## What OSC Needs to Adopt and Operationalize a Hackathon Prototype

To transition a hackathon prototype into a durable City service, OSC must address governance, compliance, and technical integration.

* **Governance and Policy:**
 * *Content Standards:* Establish editorial style, neutrality guidelines, and an approval workflow via the existing PIO network (e.g., Michael Hinkle for Planning, Nykita Doggette for General Services) [10].
 * *Accessibility:* Enforce WCAG 2.1 AA standards, Spanish language parity, ASL/video captioning, and alt-text requirements, mirroring the inclusive standards set by the RVAPB rulebook [6].
 * *Legal/Records:* Ensure FOIA retention compliance [10], align takedown/deaccession policies with the Public Art Commission [13], and implement standard story release/consent forms.
* **Technology and Security:**
 * *Integration:* Build CMS integration for rva.gov, enable RSS/JSON ingestion from partner departments, and implement schema.org/Event markup for SEO.
 * *Privacy:* Adopt privacy-by-design principles, ensuring no unnecessary Personally Identifiable Information (PII) is collected, similar to RVA311's anonymous submission options [5].
* **Operations:**
 * *RACI Matrix:* Define clear roles across OSC (Product Owner), IT (Tech Lead), PIOs (Content Approvers), and departments (Feed Suppliers).
 * *Metrics:* Track unique visitors, event click-through rates, language parity rates, and 311-to-arts conversion rates.
* **Procurement:**
 * If external code is used, ensure vendor onboarding, data processing addendums, and source code escrow are established.

## How the City Currently Promotes Arts Events vs. How It Could

Moving from a siloed, press-driven model to a unified, feed-driven model will exponentially increase arts visibility.

| Feature | Current State (Today) | Future State (Proposed Hub) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Discovery Surface** | Scattered across department pages and off-domain sites (e.g., rvapb.org) [7] [3]. | Centralized at `rva.gov/arts` with canonical URLs. |
| **Event Promotion** | Ad hoc press releases and siloed social media posts [14]. | Auto-ingested feeds, ICS calendar exports, and co-branded *Hey, Richmond* drops. |
| **Cross-Promotion** | 311 used strictly for service requests (potholes, taxes) [5]. | 311 web/app features geo-targeted "What's On Near You" arts banners. |
| **Language Access** | Generic Google Translate widget on rva.gov [5]. | Native bilingual assets (English/Spanish/ASL) matching PB standards [6]. |
| **Public Art Updates** | Requires sign-up to a separate Richmond 300 Mailchimp list [3]. | Live PAC project map and RFQ feed embedded directly in the arts hub. |

*Takeaway:* A centralized hub transforms arts promotion from a manual, fragmented chore into an automated, resident-centric service.

## Department Roles and Incentives

To make cross-department collaboration durable, each stakeholder must understand their role and incentive:
* **OSC:** Acts as the distributor and editorial lead. *Incentive:* Higher engagement rates for City newsletters and social channels.
* **Planning / PAC:** Supplies the public art pipeline and RFQ data. *Incentive:* Broader visibility for the 1% for art program and easier compliance with public notice requirements [3].
* **Parks & Recreation:** Supplies venue and program data. *Incentive:* Increased attendance at community centers and workshops [4].
* **Budget & Strategic Planning:** Connects Engage RVA and PB links. *Incentive:* Higher participation in budget town halls and PB voting [6] [8].
* **Office of Neighborhood Engagement:** Acts as neighborhood connectors. *Incentive:* A platform to highlight grassroots community leaders [12].

## Implementation Roadmap

A 180-day path to value ensures rapid deployment and measurable lift:
* **Phase 1: Discover (Weeks 1-4):** Verify champion roles within OSC. Audit existing PAC and PRCF data feeds. Draft content accessibility checklists.
* **Phase 2: Build (Weeks 5-10):** Develop the `rva.gov/arts` MVP. Integrate the PAC project map [3] and establish canonical URLs for off-domain tools.
* **Phase 3: Pilot (Weeks 11-14):** Launch the hub internally. Run a limited RVA311 promo test tied to two district events. Publish the first "Neighborhood Arts Spotlight" featuring the Poet Laureate [2].
* **Phase 4: Iterate (Weeks 15-18):** Review 311 conversion metrics and language parity SLA compliance. Refine the resident story intake portal.
* **Phase 5: Scale (Weeks 19-26):** Public launch co-branded with the *Hey, Richmond* newsletter. Expand feed ingestion to local libraries and external community partners.

## Risks and Mitigations

* **Risk:** Accessibility gaps and lack of language parity alienate residents.
 * *Mitigation:* Implement pre-publication checklists enforcing WCAG 2.1 AA and Spanish/ASL requirements, modeled on the RVAPB rulebook [6].
* **Risk:** Off-domain drift continues, eroding trust and FOIA compliance.
 * *Mitigation:* Mandate that all external surveys (Eventbrite, ArcGIS) use canonical `go.rva.gov` shortlinks and route initial sign-ups through rva.gov [8] [9].
* **Risk:** Low partner adoption from other departments.
 * *Mitigation:* Establish clear MOUs and provide simple, automated feed templates (RSS/JSON) so departments do not have to duplicate data entry.
