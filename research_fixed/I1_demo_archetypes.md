
# Winning 90-Second Civic Demos: Show Working Value Fast for Richmond

## Executive Summary
In civic tech hackathons, the difference between a winning pitch and a forgotten concept lies in the ability to demonstrate working value within the first 90 seconds. Research into civic tech judging rubrics reveals a universal truth: judges heavily penalize abstract "Fog" pitches and reward concrete, working prototypes. For the upcoming Richmond Civic Hackathon (Hack for RVA), teams must prioritize live, working evidence tied directly to local outcomes. 

**Key Strategic Actions:**
* **Default to a "Paint-by-Numbers" walkthrough:** Open on live screens and complete a real user task in under 60 seconds. Code for America, NYC BigApps, and Richmond's own rubrics all explicitly require working demos or prototypes.
* **Align immediately with local impact:** Hack for RVA awards $10,000 in prizes tied to the Mayor's Action Plan (MAP) pillars. Name the pillar in your first sentence and use real Richmond data (e.g., actual venue names, real neighborhood statistics) to prove feasibility.
* **Adopt a multi-asset strategy:** Produce a single storyboard that can be cut into a 90-second live pitch, a 1-minute screen recording, and a 2-3 minute submission video to satisfy all judging formats efficiently.
* **Match the archetype to the domain:** Use a pure Paint-by-Numbers approach for Arts Discovery to show immediate utility, and a hybrid Film-then-Paint approach for Story Collection to balance emotional resonance with technical credibility.

## Decision Context: Richmond Judges Need Working, MAP-Aligned Impact Fast

### Working Prototypes Outscore Conceptual Pitches
Public criteria and historical precedents show that civic tech judges prioritize live, working value over conceptual pitches. The failure mode for many teams is the "Fog" archetype—high-abstraction language that never resolves to a specific user action. Frameworks that do not resolve to a working product score poorly on execution and violate baseline submission requirements.
* **Code for America Summit Demo Lab:** Requires a "One-minute screen recording" that explicitly "showcases your demo in action" [1]. (URL: https://summit.codeforamerica.org/demo-lab/)
* **NYC BigApps:** Mandated that participants "Create a working Software Application" and submit a video and photograph of the "working software Application" [2]. (URL: https://nycbigapps2010.devpost.com/rules)
* **Technical.ly Civic Hackathon Guidelines:** Judges evaluate projects on a 1-5 scale based on whether the "demo or presentation suggest that the project has reached a stage of completion" [3]. (URL: https://gist.github.com/mheadd/4195641)
* **Richmond Civic Hackathon (Hack for RVA):** Submissions require a "Working prototype or demo" and a "Demo video (2–3 minutes recommended)" [4]. (URL: https://hack-for-rva.devpost.com/?ref_feature=challenge&ref_medium=discover)

### Local Impact and Execution Drive Richmond Scoring
Hack for RVA is a 48-hour civic hackathon offering $10,000 in awards, including a Mayor's Choice Award and seven prizes aligned with the Mayor's Action Plan (MAP) pillars [5]. Judges will evaluate projects based on Impact, Innovation, Feasibility, Execution, and Collaboration [4]. 

To score highly on "Execution" and "Impact," teams must avoid wireframes during the live demo. Every claim must be backed by a visible click, filter, or data point. For arts discovery, this means preloading true Richmond event data; for storytelling, it means showing a Richmond planner's dashboard with concrete, localized counts.

## What Great Looks Like: Proven Demo Patterns to Copy

### Short, Structured Demos Outperform Meandering Narratives
Proven product marketing guidance recommends a brief hook, a tight walkthrough, and a quick outcome recap. 
* **Show-Tell-Show Framework:** Start with the outcome the audience wants to see, outline the steps using real screens, and conclude by revisiting the outcome to reinforce the value [6]. 
* **The Journey Framework:** Plan a "big question" that the demo will answer, envision life without the product, and then show the journey from challenge to solution [7].
* **The 60-Second Hero's Journey:** Identify the hero, describe their challenge, articulate the pain, share what happened when they used the product, and finish with how it changed their job for the better [8].

### The Paint-by-Numbers Benchmark
A prime example of a successful Paint-by-Numbers civic tech demo is the Marcy Lab School's "TenantVoice" presentation. The demo features a narrated, step-by-step UI walkthrough using real data. The presenter narrates the persona's journey (e.g., logging in, navigating, submitting a report, and visualizing data) with concrete, on-screen outputs [9] [10]. Teams should replicate this persona + task + on-screen proof structure.

## Archetype Selection: Pick by Domain, Not Taste

Hackathon demos fall into recognizable archetypes. Selecting the right one prevents judges from spending their 90 seconds waiting to understand the product. 

### Arts Discovery: Discover Richmond Arts Events Fast
For arts discovery, the Paint-by-Numbers archetype with real event data is the strongest choice because the tool's value is immediately obvious when working data is present.

| Archetype | Emotional Resonance | Technical Credibility | 90-Sec Achievability | Risk of Misfire | Recommended Use Case | Do Not Do This Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paint-by-Numbers** | Medium | High | High | Low | Resident searches "visual arts" + "this weekend," sees 10 real Richmond events with venue/date/source link. | Saying "we aggregate all arts" while showing empty results or dummy placeholder data. |
| **Film** | High (if vivid) | Medium | Medium | Medium | 15–30 sec vignette about weekend planning, then immediate live search → list. | 90 seconds of lifestyle B-roll and values with no on-screen search/results. |
| **Fog** | Low | Low | High (to talk) | High | None—fails working demo expectations. | Vision deck + wireframes; no live data; "cultural equity" platitudes only. |
| **Quest** | Medium | Medium | Medium | Medium-High | "Can we find a kid-friendly visual arts event within 30 seconds?" live mission. | Open-ended browsing across multiple tabs with unpredictable data paths. |

*Takeaway: Default to Paint-by-Numbers. Execute a deterministic, pre-validated search path with authentic local data to prove immediate utility.*

### Story Collection: Collect, Tag, and Surface Resident Stories
For story collection, a hybrid Film-then-Paint structure—30 seconds of narrative framing followed by 60 seconds of tool walkthrough—balances emotional resonance with demonstrable evidence.

| Archetype | Emotional Resonance | Technical Credibility | 90-Sec Achievability | Risk of Misfire | Recommended Use Case | Do Not Do This Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paint-by-Numbers** | Medium | High | High | Low | Capture a story live → auto-tag → dashboard shows "14 of 40 mention Hull Street parking." | Walking through admin/config screens instead of capturing a real story. |
| **Film** | High | Medium | Medium | Medium | 20–30 sec resident vignette to humanize stakes, then jump to live dashboard proof. | Entire 90 seconds on narrative without showing any product or data. |
| **Fog** | High (language only) | Low | High (to talk) | High | None—penalized by "stage of completion" criteria. | "AI platform translates lived experience..." + diagram, no demo. |
| **Quest** | High | Medium | Medium | Medium-High | "Collect one story and see it appear on the map in under a minute." | Attempting multi-user workflows (moderation + analytics + export) in 90 seconds. |

*Takeaway: Use Film-then-Paint. Humanize the problem for 20-30 seconds, then prove the solution with a live capture and a visible dashboard delta.*

## Arts Discovery Playbook: 90 Seconds to Real Richmond Events

To succeed in the Arts Discovery domain, teams must execute a deterministic, pre-validated search path. 

### The 15-60-15 Script Structure
* **0–15s (Hook):** "MAP Pillar: Arts access. It's Friday. A Richmond resident types 'visual arts' + 'this weekend'..."
* **15–75s (Live Walkthrough):** Enter terms → apply date filter → display 10 events with venue/date/link → click one to open the source page.
* **75–90s (Recap):** Recap the outcome; name the venues and dates; call out data freshness.

### Data Reliability and Risk Mitigation
Preload 10+ verified events from trusted local sources and cache them to avoid live latency. To mitigate risks, seed a fallback query in case of empty results, prepare a static snapshot overlay for slow load times, and periodically re-verify links to prevent stale data during the live pitch.

## Story Collection Playbook: Film-then-Paint in 90 Seconds

Story collection requires emotional stakes, but those stakes must quickly transition into technical proof.

### The 30-60 Script Structure
* **0–30s (Film):** "Church Hill is changing fast—residents' stories are invisible in decisions."
* **30–90s (Paint):** Submit one story live → show tags auto-apply → switch to dashboard updating to "14 of 40 mention parking" → show planner view filtering to Hull Street.

### Evidence Targets and Risk Mitigation
The demo must show a real-time count change and surface a filterable tag. If time permits, show an export or permalink ready for handoff. To mitigate risks, allow a manual tag toggle in case NLP mis-tags the live entry, use a local debounce with a visible spinner to mask latency, and ensure all demo data redacts Personally Identifiable Information (PII).

## Asset Plan: Satisfy All Formats, One Storyboard

Hack for RVA requires a 2-3 minute video [4], while standard civic tech showcases like CfA require a 1-minute screen recording [1]. Teams should produce a single storyboard that yields three cuts:
1. **90-Second Live Pitch:** The core script for stage presentations.
2. **1-Minute Screen Recording:** A fast-paced, CfA-style "show it working" cut with no filler.
3. **2-3 Minute Devpost Video:** The full 90-second pitch bookended by 10-second context bumpers and expanded technical explanations.

## Rehearsal and Reliability: Execution is Judged

Because Richmond explicitly scores "Execution: Quality of the prototype and presentation" [4], teams must optimize for flow over architecture. Prioritize fast page loads, deterministic paths, and clear error states. Avoid discussing backend architecture unless explicitly asked during Q&A. Conduct dry runs on event Wi-Fi, keep a pre-recorded backup video ready, and prepare A/B scripts depending on data volatility.

## Risk Register and Unverified Elements

While public rubrics provide a strong baseline, certain specific event details remain unverified publicly. The exact weighted judging rubric (e.g., specific scoring weights for Impact vs. Execution) and the precise live pitch format (e.g., strict 90 seconds vs. extended Q&A) are not currently available. To hedge these risks, teams should balance emotional framing with hard technical proof and prepare modular scripts that can expand or contract as needed on event day.

## Action Plan and Timeline: 7-Day Sprint to Demo-Ready

To ensure a flawless execution, follow this 7-day sprint schedule:
* **Day 1:** Pick the archetype per domain; confirm the persona and tasks; gather authentic Richmond data.
* **Day 2:** Draft scripts and storyboards; define on-screen success metrics.
* **Day 3–4:** Build deterministic demo paths; cache data; instrument dashboards.
* **Day 5:** Record the 1-minute and 2-3 minute video cuts.
* **Day 6:** Conduct live rehearsals under 90 seconds; run failure drills (e.g., Wi-Fi drops).
* **Day 7:** Final QA; upload all assets to Devpost; print cheat sheets for the live pitch.
