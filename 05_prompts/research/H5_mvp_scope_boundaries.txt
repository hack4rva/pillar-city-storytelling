Section H — MVP Feasibility
Title: Scope Limits for City Storytelling MVPs in 48 Hours

Understanding what is explicitly out of scope is as important as knowing what to build. For arts event discovery, the following features are out of scope for a 48-hour prototype: comprehensive coverage of all Richmond arts events (coverage will necessarily be partial and should be disclosed as such); deep venue profiles with photos, hours, accessibility information, and parking; ticketing integration that allows in-tool purchase or reservation; official City of Richmond endorsement or affiliation branding; user accounts, personalization, or saved events; mobile-native applications; and automated deduplication across all sources. Teams sometimes pursue comprehensive coverage or official-seeming branding because they want their tool to feel authoritative. The risk is that the quest for completeness consumes the build time needed to get even the core data pipeline working. A demo showing 20 real Richmond events with correct dates, venues, and links is more compelling than a beautiful empty interface that claims it "will aggregate all Richmond arts events."

For story collection tools, the out-of-scope list is even more important because scope creep tends toward technically exciting but ethically risky features. Out of scope in 48 hours: full audio or video transcription pipeline at scale (a transcription demo for one pre-recorded audio clip is achievable, but an at-scale pipeline is not); AI-powered policy recommendations derived from story themes (theme extraction is achievable, but claiming the tool generates policy recommendations conflates correlation with analysis and creates false authority); integration with City planning, zoning, or communications systems (requires official City partnerships and system access that do not exist); verified or legally archived story repositories (requires institutional infrastructure beyond a hackathon); full content moderation with human review queue (the demo should acknowledge moderation is needed in production). Teams that pitch any of these features as present in their demo should expect skeptical judges — and should deserve skeptical judges.

The clearest sign that a storytelling project is over-scoped is when the team cannot describe its core user value in one sentence. "A platform that aggregates Richmond arts events from multiple sources into one filterable calendar" is one sentence. "An AI-powered civic insight engine that translates resident narratives into actionable policy intelligence through natural language processing and geographic story clustering" is not a product that can be built in 48 hours — it is a grant proposal. When teams reach for language like "ecosystem," "platform," "intelligence layer," or "comprehensive solution," they are usually avoiding the harder question of what specific user problem the demo will show being solved. Coaches and mentors should push teams to articulate the minimum viable demo: one user, one problem, one action in the tool, one outcome. Everything else is v2.
