Section G — Risks
Title: Practical Guardrails for Arts and Storytelling Civic Tech

An arts event aggregator that publishes stale, inaccurate, or cancelled event information causes direct harm — residents show up to a gallery opening that ended two weeks ago, or purchase tickets to an event that the organizer quietly cancelled. Unlike large commercial event platforms with engineering teams and enforcement mechanisms, a hackathon prototype will not have automatic systems for detecting event cancellations, venue closures, or last-minute changes. Teams must research what the most common failure modes are: Eventbrite listings left up after events pass, venues that never remove outdated events from their websites, Facebook events marked as "going" by organizers who then cancel without notification. The guardrail question is not whether these failures can be prevented entirely — they cannot — but how to make them visible and recoverable: displaying event source and retrieval date, linking back to original source rather than republishing, showing a "last verified" timestamp, and making it easy for organizations to flag incorrect information.

For story collection tools, the consent and privacy guardrails are not optional features to be added in version 2 — they are prerequisites for ethical deployment even in prototype form. The most critical guardrails are: explicit, informed consent before submission (not buried in a terms-of-service checkbox); clear explanation of who will read the stories, how they will be stored, and whether they will be made public; the ability to submit anonymously; and a clear process for withdrawing a submitted story. Teams should research what happens when story collection tools receive sensitive content — stories about housing discrimination, domestic violence, police encounters, immigration status — which are foreseeable given that neighborhood change and displacement are explicitly cited problem contexts in this pillar. A tool without a documented content moderation or triage plan is not ready for public use, even in a limited pilot.

Quality control and anti-gaming guardrails are frequently overlooked in civic tech storytelling tools. A publicly accessible story submission form will receive off-topic submissions, spam, and potentially adversarial content within hours of going live. Research how other civic story collection tools handle this: StoryCorps uses trained facilitators; the MIT Media Lab's Place Pulse used human rating; many urban planning story tools use moderated queues where staff review before publication. For arts aggregators, the analogous risk is gaming by venues that over-post or post misleading event descriptions to increase visibility. Teams should also consider what happens when AI theme extraction is applied to story collections: NLP tools that surface "themes" from resident stories can amplify statistically common narratives while suppressing minority experiences, effectively reintroducing the representational bias the tool was meant to address. The guardrail here is human review of AI outputs before any claim is made that a theme represents "community sentiment."
