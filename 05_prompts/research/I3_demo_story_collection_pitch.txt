Section I — Demo Guidance
Title: Demo Script for a Resident Story Collection and Civic Insight Tool

The central demo decision for a story collection tool is which user to center: the resident who submits a story, or the City staff member who reads the insights. Both are real users; the product serves both; but they require completely different demo journeys. The resident submission demo is emotionally compelling and immediately understandable — everyone can imagine what it feels like to want their neighborhood's story to be heard. The City staff insight demo is more practically powerful — it shows why the tool should exist in government infrastructure — but requires the audience to understand a less intuitive workflow. The recommended structure for a 90-second demo is to show both, in order: 30 seconds on the submission experience (emotional resonance, establishes the input), 60 seconds on the insight dashboard (practical value, establishes the output). This sequence answers both "why would a resident use this?" and "why would the City use this?" within the time limit.

Showing story content in a demo requires careful handling. If the stories are real submissions from community members (the ideal scenario), teams should have explicit consent from contributors to show their stories on screen during the demo, and should honor any anonymization requests. If the stories are from archival sources (Valentine oral history excerpts), they should be labeled as archival, not new submissions. If they are team-written composite stories, they should feel authentic and Richmond-specific — not generic ("I've lived here 30 years and have seen a lot of change") but specific ("I grew up on the 1900 block of P Street in Jackson Ward when the Hippodrome was still running, and I watched the block change three times before I stopped counting"). The specificity of story content is what makes the insight dashboard meaningful — vague stories produce vague themes, and judges will not be impressed by a theme cluster that says "change" and "community." Research what real Richmond neighborhood change stories sound like by reading existing oral history excerpts.

The most powerful insight that could emerge from a story collection dashboard — one that would make a City planner sit up in their chair — is a concrete, Richmond-specific example of a theme connecting to a real planning decision. For example: "Of the 40 pilot stories collected from Church Hill, North Side, and Manchester residents, 18 mention a specific loss of neighborhood-serving retail (grocery stores, hardware stores, laundromats) as a marker of neighborhood change — a signal that zoning and retail mix decisions have community-visible consequences that don't appear in development permit data." This is the kind of output a City planner cannot get from a survey or a public hearing transcript. Teams should research what Richmond planning decisions are currently in public comment — the Richmond 300 Master Plan implementation, Southside planning processes, Boulevard redevelopment — to identify where story-based insight is most relevant and frame the dashboard demo accordingly.
