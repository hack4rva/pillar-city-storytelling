Top 10 Research Questions — A City That Tells Its Stories

These are the most important open questions for hackathon teams working in this pillar. Answer them before committing to a project direction.

1. What arts and cultural event data is actually accessible via public API or structured feed in Richmond today? Which organizations publish Eventbrite listings? Which maintain RSS or iCal calendar feeds?

2. What does dorichmond.com currently cover, and what arts events does it miss? Where are the gaps that a more arts-focused tool could address?

3. How do Richmond residents currently discover arts and cultural events? What channels do they rely on, and where do they fail?

4. What is the current state of Facebook Events API access for third-party applications? Is any structured event data accessible without significant prior app review?

5. What oral history and neighborhood story collections exist at The Valentine, Black History Museum, VCU Libraries, and Virginia Museum of History and Culture? What is publicly accessible digitally?

6. What community organizations in Richmond are already doing story-based civic work — StoryCorps affiliates, local documentary groups, neighborhood history projects?

7. What are the most comfortable storytelling channels for older residents, non-digital users, and residents with limited English proficiency in Richmond? What has worked in comparable cities?

8. What consent and privacy frameworks are appropriate for a city-connected story collection tool? What do similar tools (StoryCorps, Historypin) use?

9. Who are the named potential champions for this pillar (Todd Waldo, Meg Hughes, Sylvio Lynch III, Chaya Braxton) and what institutional capacities do they represent for post-hackathon continuation?

10. What is the most time-sensitive civic opportunity in this pillar — arts aggregation (ongoing need) or story collection (urgent due to neighborhood change and aging residents)?
