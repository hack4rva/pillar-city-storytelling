Section I — Demo Guidance
Title: Demo Script for a Richmond Arts Event Discovery Tool

The strongest user scenario for a Richmond arts event discovery demo is someone with genuine motivation to find arts events who is frustrated by the current scattered landscape — not a hypothetical user but a recognizable Richmond archetype. Three candidate personas: (1) A person who recently moved to Richmond from another city and wants to explore its arts scene but doesn't know which websites to check, which organizations to follow, or which neighborhoods have which kinds of arts activity; (2) A local resident who wants to support Richmond artists and attend events but finds that by the time they see a Facebook post about a gallery opening, it's already over; (3) A parent looking for culturally enriching family activities who is overwhelmed by googling "Richmond arts events for kids" and getting results from five different websites with different levels of currency. The first persona is the most broadly relatable and avoids the "why not just follow the gallery on Instagram" objection from judges who are already arts-engaged Richmond residents.

Handling real versus mock event data is a key demo preparation decision. The strongest demos use real data pulled live or pulled the morning of demo day — real Richmond venue names (1708 Gallery, the Valentine, Black History Museum and Cultural Center of Virginia, Visual Arts Center of Richmond, ArtSpace, the Broadberry, Strange Matter), real upcoming dates, real event descriptions. When judges see "First Fridays at 1708 Gallery, Friday March 20, 6-9pm, 319 W Broad Street" rather than "Gallery Event, [DATE], [VENUE]," the tool immediately feels useful rather than conceptual. The risk of live data is that the API call fails during the demo — teams should cache a snapshot of current event data before presenting. If real data is thin (a slow arts week), teams should supplement with a hand-seeded dataset of confirmed upcoming Richmond events identified before the hackathon.

The most predictable judge question is: "Why would someone use this instead of just going to Do Richmond or checking Eventbrite?" Teams must prepare a crisp answer. The honest answer has two parts: (1) Do Richmond does not aggregate from all sources — it has its own curatorial calendar that requires manual submission, meaning many events are missing; (2) Eventbrite only shows Eventbrite-listed events, excluding gallery openings, community arts events, and arts activities at neighborhood institutions. The tool's value is aggregation across sources, not curation — it is the one place to look rather than the fifth. Teams should also be prepared to acknowledge what the tool does not do: it does not verify event accuracy, it does not cover every event, it does not replace arts organizations' own channels. This honesty builds more judge confidence than overclaiming.
