Section I — Demo Guidance
Title: Framing Language for City Storytelling Demos

Opening lines matter disproportionately in 90-second demos because judges form rapid impressions, and a weak opening creates a deficit the rest of the demo must overcome. For arts discovery, the strongest opening lines are specific, quantified, and immediately relatable: "Richmond's arts scene produces more than 200 events per month — gallery openings in Jackson Ward, theater in the Fan, music in Scott's Addition, cultural programming across every neighborhood — but most Richmond residents only ever find a fraction of them, because discovering them requires checking five different websites, following a dozen Instagram accounts, and hoping a friend mentions the right thing at the right time." This opening does three things in two sentences: establishes Richmond's arts vitality (positive framing that respects the community), names the specific pain point (fragmentation and discovery failure), and makes the problem feel personal (the word "hoping" signals luck-based discovery, which everyone finds frustrating). Teams should research whether a more specific and verifiable opening number exists — actual event counts from Eventbrite, VMFA, or local arts organizations — to replace "more than 200 events" with a documented figure.

For story collection, the strongest opening line makes the cost of inaction feel concrete and irreversible: "Richmond's fastest-changing neighborhoods — Church Hill, Manchester, Northside — are full of residents who have watched those places transform over decades. Their stories — what those neighborhoods felt like before, what was lost, what mattered — are not recorded anywhere. When those residents are gone, those stories are gone. And the City planning decisions that will shape those neighborhoods for the next 50 years are being made without them." This opening works because it names real Richmond neighborhoods (not generic "underserved communities"), frames the loss as irreversible (not merely inconvenient), and connects directly to a real government decision-making context (planning). Teams should avoid opening with AI or technology framing — "our AI-powered platform" — because it shifts attention to the tool before the problem has been established, and it triggers skepticism before credibility has been built.

Positioning language matters throughout the demo, not just in the opening. For arts discovery, the correct positioning is: "We are not replacing arts organizations' websites or social media — we are making them collectively visible." This framing is important for two reasons: it respects the arts organizations whose data the tool is using (reducing the "are you stealing our content?" concern), and it correctly describes the tool's actual function (aggregation, not replacement). Teams should avoid language like "one-stop shop," "the definitive Richmond arts calendar," or "official" — these phrases overclaim authority and create accountability the prototype cannot support. For story collection, the correct positioning is: "We are giving residents a dedicated space to share neighborhood stories, and giving City staff a new source of community insight — not to replace surveys or public hearings, but to complement them with the kind of specific, personal context those processes rarely capture." This framing positions the tool as additive to existing civic processes rather than competitive with them.

Impact framing is the final test of whether a demo leaves judges with a clear and honest sense of what the tool could accomplish. For arts discovery, realistic impact framing sounds like: "Artists and arts organizations gain visibility without additional admin work — they post to their existing channel, and our tool finds them. Residents discover events they would have missed. Over time, the tool builds a richer picture of where Richmond's arts activity is concentrated — and where it isn't." This is honest, modest, and specific enough to be believable. For story collection, realistic framing sounds like: "City planners have one new source of community insight alongside surveys and public hearings — not to replace those processes, but to surface the kinds of place-specific, long-horizon stories that structured feedback forms never elicit. The goal is not to automate policy, but to make invisible community knowledge visible." Teams that use this framing signal maturity to judges who have seen enough civic tech overpromising to be appropriately skeptical.

Output
- Facts (with URLs): Research whether any verified Richmond arts event volume statistics exist (from VMFA, Richmond Department of Arts Culture and Heritage, or Greater Richmond arts economic impact studies) that could replace estimated figures in opening lines; identify whether the Richmond 300 Master Plan or any official City document explicitly acknowledges limitations in how community voice is collected during planning processes.
- Inferences (clearly labeled): [Inference] Opening with a specific, verifiable Richmond statistic is significantly more persuasive to judges than opening with an estimated or rounded figure, even if the estimate is directionally accurate. [Inference] Framing arts discovery as "making existing arts organizations collectively visible" rather than "building a new arts platform" is more likely to earn endorsement from arts community members on judging panels.
- Unknowns (what cannot be verified publicly): Whether any Richmond City Council members, planning staff, or Department of Arts leadership have publicly expressed interest in story-based community input tools, which would strengthen the "City would use this" framing if cited.
- Produce a framing language guide for both tools, organized as three sections: (1) Opening lines — two options each, one data-led and one narrative-led, with notes on when to use each; (2) Positioning statements — two sentences each that correctly bound the tool's scope and relationship to existing systems; (3) Closing impact statements — two sentences each that make a realistic, honest, specific impact claim. Flag any phrases that should be avoided (overclaiming, technology-first, vague civic language) with brief explanations of why.
