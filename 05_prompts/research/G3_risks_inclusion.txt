Section G — Risks
Title: Inclusion and Accessibility Risks in Arts Discovery and Storytelling Tools

Richmond's digital divide is not evenly distributed. Census data shows that broadband access in neighborhoods like Gilpin Court, Creighton Court, and portions of the East End lags significantly behind the city average. Residents in these neighborhoods — many of whom are also the communities most underrepresented in civic technology products — are precisely those who would benefit most from improved arts discovery and storytelling tools, yet are most at risk of being left out. A smartphone-dependent arts discovery tool that requires a Google account or Eventbrite login to access event data effectively draws a boundary around who counts as a cultural participant. Teams must research what share of Richmond's lower-income and elderly residents rely on feature phones, shared devices, or public library internet access — and design accordingly.

The geography of Richmond's arts infrastructure itself creates a built-in bias risk. Most arts organizations with maintained websites, active social media, and Eventbrite presence are clustered in Scott's Addition, Carytown, Shockoe Slip, and the Museum District. Neighborhood arts programming in the East End, Northside, and South Richmond — including community theater, church-based cultural events, spoken word nights at barbershops and community centers — is far less likely to have a digital footprint compatible with API aggregation. An event aggregator that only pulls from Eventbrite and venue websites will systematically underrepresent Black-led cultural programming and neighborhood-scale events. This is not a bug easily patched after launch; it is a structural problem that must be acknowledged and mitigated during design.

Story collection tools carry their own distinct inclusion risks. Voice-based or text submission tools assume literacy, language proficiency in English, comfort with online forms, and a baseline of trust in digital platforms — assumptions that do not hold uniformly across Richmond's diverse population. Older residents who carry the most irreplaceable neighborhood history may be the least likely to submit stories through a web form. Non-English-speaking communities, including Richmond's growing Latinx and Vietnamese populations, face language barriers that most civic tech tools ignore entirely. There is also a subtler risk: story collection tools that rely on self-selection will amplify voices that are already comfortable with civic engagement — residents who have participated in planning processes, attended public meetings, or are already connected to cultural institutions — while systematically missing the voices of residents most affected by displacement, disinvestment, and institutional neglect. Teams should research what inclusive outreach looks like in practice: oral history organizations like the Valentine use trained interviewers and community partnerships precisely because self-submission tools alone cannot reach the full range of community voices.

Output
- Facts (with URLs): Document Richmond broadband access rates by neighborhood/zip code from FCC or Census data; identify Richmond arts organizations with no active website or minimal digital presence; document share of Richmond population over 65 by neighborhood; identify whether RPS or Richmond Public Library has digital literacy programs that could serve as distribution partners.
- Inferences (clearly labeled): [Inference] An arts aggregator built solely on Eventbrite and venue website scraping will likely underrepresent at least 30-40% of Richmond's active cultural programming, particularly events in majority-Black neighborhoods. [Inference] A web-form-only story submission tool will skew submissions toward residents under 60 with broadband access.
- Unknowns (what cannot be verified publicly): Whether specific Richmond arts organizations serving lower-income communities have any accessible digital event calendar; whether Richmond's Office of Multicultural Affairs or immigrant services organizations have formally documented the digital access gaps for non-English-speaking residents in civic participation tools.
- Provide a risk matrix with two axes: population group (elderly, non-English speakers, low-income, disability) vs. tool type (arts aggregator, web form story collection, voice story collection) — rating inclusion risk as low/medium/high for each cell, with one mitigation strategy per high-risk cell.
