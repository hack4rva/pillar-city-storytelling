Section I — Demo Guidance
Title: Demo Clarity Archetypes for Arts Discovery and Storytelling Tools

Hackathon demos fall into recognizable archetypes, and understanding which archetype fits which civic tech product is the difference between judges who understand immediately and judges who spend the 90 seconds waiting to understand. The Paint-by-Numbers archetype is the strongest for tools with a clear UI and real data: show the thing working, step by step, narrating what the user is doing and why. For arts discovery, this means: "Here is our tool. A Richmond resident opens it Friday morning wondering what arts events are happening this weekend. She types 'visual arts' and selects 'this weekend' — and here are the results, ten real events happening in Richmond over the next three days, with venue, date, and a direct link to the original event page." Judges who see real event data with real Richmond venue names and real upcoming dates understand the value within 20 seconds.

The Film archetype — telling a narrative arc — is particularly suited for civic storytelling tools where the emotional value is as important as the functional value. For a story collection tool, the narrative arc might be: "Richmond's Church Hill neighborhood has changed faster in the last decade than at any point since the 1970s. Long-time residents have stories about that change — but today those stories exist only in individual memories and scattered social media posts, invisible to the City planners making decisions about the neighborhood's future. Our tool creates a space for those stories to be collected and heard." The Film archetype works when the "before" state is vivid and painful enough that the product's value is obvious by contrast. The risk is that a Film-style demo can become the Fog archetype — all evocative framing, no demonstrable product — if the team has not built far enough to show the actual tool working.

The Fog archetype is the failure mode for both problem domains in this pillar. Arts discovery Fog looks like: "Imagine a world where every Richmond resident can discover the full richness of the city's arts scene, where artists gain visibility and audiences, where cultural equity becomes a reality..." followed by a wireframe. Story collection Fog looks like: "Our AI platform translates the lived experiences of Richmond residents into actionable civic intelligence, bridging the gap between community voice and policy decision-making..." followed by a diagram. Fog demos are characterized by high-abstraction language that never resolves to a specific user action in a working tool. Judges who see Fog after 90 seconds have learned nothing about whether the product works. Coaches should train teams to replace every abstract phrase with a specific, concrete demo moment: replace "bridging community voice and policy" with "a City planner opens the dashboard Monday morning and sees that 14 of the 40 submitted stories mention parking and access on Hull Street."
