Section G — Risks
Title: Copyright, Attribution, and Content Rights Risks

Arts organizations and individual artists retain copyright over their promotional materials — event descriptions, photographs, artwork images, press releases — regardless of where those materials are published online. When a hackathon team builds an event aggregator that pulls text and images from gallery websites, Facebook event pages, Eventbrite listings, or Richmond arts media like RVA Magazine and Style Weekly, they are copying and republishing copyrighted content without explicit license. For a prototype demo this may not trigger enforcement, but a live product would be exposed to takedown requests, particularly from arts organizations that feel their brand or materials are being misrepresented. Teams should research what terms of service apply to the most common Richmond arts data sources — Eventbrite's public API, Facebook's Graph API (which heavily restricted Events access in 2018), gallery RSS feeds, and iCal exports — and understand which access methods imply permission to republish versus merely index.

The attribution question is particularly complex for arts events. A single event listing may involve a venue (owns the space), a presenting organization (organized the event), individual artists (created the work), and a ticketing platform (hosts the listing). An aggregator that surfaces this event without clear attribution could misattribute the event to the wrong organization, omit artist credit entirely, or present the aggregator itself as an authoritative source rather than a secondary index. Richmond arts organizations are small enough that misattribution causes real reputational harm — a gallery incorrectly listed as closed, an artist credited to the wrong venue, a ticket link that goes to a defunct Eventbrite page. Teams should research how established arts aggregators like Do Richmond, ArtsRVA (if active), or national platforms like Goldstar handle attribution legally and practically, and what disclaimers they use.

For story collection tools, the intellectual property questions are different but equally significant. When a resident submits a personal story — a narrative of their neighborhood's changes, a memory of a community institution — that story is their intellectual property. The tool operator acquires whatever rights the submission form grants, which is why the consent and licensing language in submission forms matters enormously. Teams should research what standard practice looks like in civic oral history projects: the Valentine Museum's oral history collection uses explicit deed-of-gift agreements specifying how recordings and transcripts can be used, by whom, and for how long. Other cities' civic storytelling projects — including MIT's CoUrbanize, Esri's Story Maps community projects, and the StoryCorps model — have developed standard approaches to story rights. Teams should document the minimum consent language required for a story collection prototype to be used ethically in a civic context, including whether contributors should retain the right to withdraw their story and whether anonymization should be offered by default.
