Section I — Demo Guidance
Title: Establishing Credibility for City Storytelling Demos

Credibility in a civic tech demo is earned through specificity, not scale. A team that says "Richmond has a thriving arts scene with hundreds of events per month but residents struggle to find them" is making a plausible but unverified claim. A team that says "Richmond has over 40 active arts organizations in the Greater Richmond area, produces over 200 arts events per month across venues from the Fan to Church Hill, but has no single aggregated event calendar — residents rely on five or six separate websites and social media accounts to piece together what's happening" is making the same point with enough specificity to feel credible. For arts discovery demos, credibility evidence includes: showing the scattered current landscape (a screenshot of 5 different websites a resident would have to check), citing real Richmond arts attendance or engagement statistics if available, and naming real Richmond arts organizations and venues throughout the demo. Research what Richmond-specific arts statistics exist: the Greater Richmond Partnership, Virginia Museum of Fine Arts annual attendance figures, ArtsRVA or similar organizations' reports on arts participation.

For story collection demos, the credibility question is more historically grounded. The risk of not capturing resident stories is not hypothetical in Richmond — it has happened repeatedly. The Valentine Museum of History is one of the country's oldest history museums and has been actively collecting Richmond oral histories for decades, yet still documents significant gaps in narratives from specific communities and neighborhoods, particularly for periods of urban renewal and highway construction in the 1950s-70s. Jackson Ward, once called the "Harlem of the South" and one of the most significant Black commercial and cultural centers in the United States, has documented histories from institutional sources but far fewer recorded oral accounts from the residents who lived through its transformation. Church Hill and Northside neighborhoods are currently experiencing rapid demographic change, and the oral accounts of long-time residents are not being systematically captured anywhere. Teams can make this credibility case by referencing the Valentine's collection gaps, the Richmond 300 Master Plan's acknowledgment of community input limitations, or specific recent Richmond development decisions where resident voices were formally solicited but informally underrepresented.

Technical credibility signals matter to judges who are evaluating whether a prototype could actually be built into a real product. For arts discovery: judges who notice a real API response (JSON data visible in a network tab, a "last updated" timestamp, event URLs that actually work) have technical confidence the pipeline is real. For story collection: judges who see a real form submission that produces a visible record in a database, and a dashboard that updates when a story is added, have confidence the system works. The temptation to hardcode demo data is strong under time pressure — and for a first demo pass, it is acceptable — but teams should flag clearly if data is hardcoded: "For this demo we seeded 15 stories; in production this would accumulate from resident submissions." Judges who discover hardcoded data that was presented as live data lose trust quickly.

Output
- Facts (with URLs): Find VMFA annual visitor or event attendance figures (vmfa.museum or their annual reports); find whether ArtsRVA, Greater Richmond Partnership, or Virginia Tourism Corporation has published Richmond arts participation or economic impact statistics; find the Valentine Museum's explicit statement about oral history collection gaps or community documentation priorities (check their website's oral history program pages).
- Inferences (clearly labeled): [Inference] A demo that cites at least two verifiable Richmond-specific statistics about arts participation or neighborhood change will be perceived as more research-grounded and credible than a demo relying solely on the team's framing. [Inference] Naming real Richmond arts districts (Scott's Addition, Carytown, Jackson Ward, Manchester, the Fan) and real institutions (1708 Gallery, Black History Museum, the Valentine) throughout the demo consistently signals local knowledge and investment to Richmond-based judges.
- Unknowns (what cannot be verified publicly): Whether the Richmond City government has formally acknowledged any gaps in community story or oral history collection as part of the Richmond 300 planning process; current attendance or engagement figures for Do Richmond or similar local arts aggregators.
- Produce a "credibility evidence card" for each of the two problem domains, listing: 3-5 specific facts that establish the problem is real and local (with recommended sources), 2-3 specific Richmond location or institution names that should appear in the demo, 1 historical reference point that demonstrates the stakes, and 1 technical signal that demonstrates the prototype is real. Keep each card to one page.
